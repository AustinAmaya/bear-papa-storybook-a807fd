The Great Tree Race

---

---

Preamble

The afternoon was golden.

Bear was sitting on the deck steps, turning his blue notebook over and over in his hands. He liked how the cover felt — smooth on one side, bumpy on the other. He wasn't writing anything. He was just thinking.

Papa was nearby, trimming the tall grass at the edge of the yard where it met the Whispering Woods.

Little Red Bear sat on the deck railing, watching.

"You look like you're thinking very hard," Little Red Bear said.

"I am," said Bear.

"What about?"

Bear looked at the Whispering Woods. The tall trees swayed a little, even though there wasn't much wind. They always did that. It was one of the things Bear liked about them.

"Running," Bear said.

Then — THWUMP.

Something landed on the grass behind him.

Bear turned around.

It was Cheetah.

She was crouched low, spotted tail flicking, golden eyes bright. She had the biggest grin Bear had ever seen on anyone.

"I HEARD that," she said.

Bear laughed. "You always do."

"Running," Cheetah said, drawing the word out slowly, like it was delicious. "Through *those* trees." She nodded at the Whispering Woods. "Race me."

Bear looked at the trees. He looked at Cheetah. He looked at the trees again.

"Papa," he called. "Cheetah wants to race through the Whispering Woods."

Papa set down his clippers. He walked over, wiping his hands on his trousers. He looked at Cheetah with calm, steady eyes.

"Through the whole woods, or just to the old oak and back?"

"Just to the old oak," Cheetah said, very innocently.

Papa's eyebrow went up.

"The old oak," he said slowly, "or the clearing with the flat stone?"

Cheetah's grin got even bigger. "...Maybe the flat stone."

---

Chapter 1

Bear had been to the flat stone before.

Papa called it the Observation Stone. It sat in a small clearing where the trees opened up and you could see a patch of night sky — even in the daytime, the gap in the canopy was easy to spot. A circle was carved into the stone's surface, with a line pointing straight up. Papa had told him someone put it there a long, long time ago.

Bear thought about that sometimes. Who put it there? Why?

But right now, Cheetah was stretching her legs. All four of them. She stretched in ways that didn't look comfortable but apparently were.

"No head start," Bear said.

"I would *never*," Cheetah said.

"You always ask for a head start."

"That's different. *This* time I won't."

Papa stood at the edge of the yard and held up one big, dark-brown hand.

"Here are the rules," he said. "Stay on the path. Go around the blackberry brambles — don't go through them. First one to touch the Observation Stone wins."

"And back?" Bear asked.

"And back."

Cheetah bounced on her front paws. She was practically vibrating.

Bear felt something bright and fizzy in his chest. He stood up straight and shook out his hands — his hands, not paws; Bear always noticed that difference — and looked at the path into the trees.

"Ready," he said.

"READY," said Cheetah, much louder.

"Go," said Papa.

They went.

---

Chapter 2

The Whispering Woods were different when you ran through them.

Bear had walked these paths many times. But running was different. Everything was closer and faster. The roots looked bigger. The light came through the leaves in moving patches, and the patches moved with him.

The trees whispered.

They always whispered. Bear had asked Papa once what the sound was. Papa said it was air moving through the needles on the pine trees, and through the gaps in the bark of the old oaks. The wood itself made a tiny, tiny sound.

But it *felt* like whispering.

Cheetah was ahead of him. Of course she was. She was a cheetah. Her spots blurred as she wove between the trees. But she kept looking back at Bear, and that slowed her down. She liked to *see* that he was there.

Bear knew the path better than Cheetah did.

He knew there was a shortcut: a tight gap between two leaning pines that the main path curved around. It was quicker if you didn't mind ducking.

He ducked.

The gap smelled like pine sap and cool shadow. Bear squeezed through and came out just ahead of the bramble curve — right as Cheetah rounded it from the long way.

She skidded to a stop. Her eyes went wide.

"How—"

"I know the path," Bear said, grinning.

He ran.

---

Chapter 3

The clearing came up fast.

Bear burst out of the trees and the light hit him all at once — brighter here, where the canopy opened up. He could see the gap in the trees that Papa used to look at the Shepherd Star on clear nights. The gap was shaped almost like a doorway.

And there was the Observation Stone.

Flat. Wide. The carved circle with the line pointing up.

Bear ran to it and slapped both hands on the stone.

"FIRST!" he shouted.

He was breathing hard. His legs felt like warm noodles. He turned around.

Cheetah came flying out of the trees about four seconds later. She was going so fast she nearly skidded past the clearing entirely. She caught herself on a tree trunk, panting, her tail straight up.

"You *cheated*," she said.

"I did not! There's no rule about the gap."

Cheetah looked at the gap between the leaning pines. She looked at Bear. She looked at the gap again.

"There should be a rule about the gap," she said.

Bear laughed. He put his hand on the Observation Stone. It was warm from the sun, even under the trees. The carved circle was rough under his fingers.

"I always wonder," Bear said, "who made this."

Cheetah padded over and looked at the carving. Her tail swished.

"Maybe it was somebody just like you," she said. "Somebody who liked looking up."

Bear looked up at the canopy gap. Just blue sky now, in the afternoon. But he knew at night the Shepherd Star would sit right there. Right in that gap.

---

Chapter 4

They sat on the Observation Stone side by side.

Cheetah's breathing slowed down. Bear's did too. It was quieter in the clearing than in the rest of the woods. The whispering of the trees was softer here, like the trees were giving them space.

"I like it here," Cheetah said.

"Me too," said Bear.

"Tell me about the star."

Bear looked at the sky gap. "It's called the Shepherd Star. Papa found it first. Well — he found it *again*. Someone was watching it a long time ago too. Before Papa was born even."

"The person who made this stone?"

"Maybe." Bear ran his finger along the carved line — straight up, pointing at the gap. "Papa says the star sometimes gets covered by a cloud. A special kind. We watched it last time."

"A cloud that goes to the star?"

"A cloud that *passes* it. Slowly. Papa said it moves toward the woods when it comes." Bear looked at the treeline. "Toward *here*."

Cheetah was quiet for a moment.

"That's spooky," she said.

"It's not spooky," Bear said. "It's a mystery."

"Spooky and mysteries are the same thing."

Bear thought about this. "Maybe," he said. "But mysteries can also be solved."

Cheetah bumped his shoulder with hers. "We should solve it."

"Someday," Bear said.

They sat in the warm quiet for a while. A bird called from somewhere above them. The trees whispered.

Then Bear stood up and stretched his arms.

"Race back?" he said.

"OBVIOUSLY," said Cheetah. She was already crouching.

"No using the gap," Bear said quickly.

"There *should* be a rule about the gap," Cheetah agreed seriously. "Starting now."

---

Chapter 5

The race back was even better than the race in.

Because this time Cheetah *won*.

She blazed ahead right from the start and Bear laughed so hard he could barely run straight. She hit the edge of the yard at full speed and did a sliding stop in the grass that left two long skid marks.

Papa was waiting there, sitting on the deck steps with Little Red Bear.

"Who won?" Papa asked.

"I DID!" Cheetah announced. "Fairly. Legally. No gaps."

"No gaps," Bear confirmed, still catching his breath. "She won fair."

Cheetah sat down next to Papa and panted happily. Bear sat on his other side. They were all warm and tired and the afternoon light was going golden.

"Good race?" Papa asked.

"We stopped at the Observation Stone," Bear said.

Papa looked at him. Not surprised, exactly. More like... knowing.

"How did it feel?" Papa asked.

"Warm," Bear said. "The stone was warm. And I thought about—" He stopped.

"About who made it?" Papa asked quietly.

Bear nodded.

Papa put one big dark-brown arm around Bear. "Me too," he said. "Every time."

Little Red Bear spoke from the railing, very quietly: "It's good to wonder about things."

Bear leaned into Papa's side. The Whispering Woods were behind them, the trees sighing their soft sound. The sky above was turning the color of peaches.

Bear thought: *Someday we'll figure it out.*

And that was enough, for now.

*The End.*
