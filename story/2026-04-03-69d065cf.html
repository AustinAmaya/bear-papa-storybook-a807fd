At home, Bear was tucked into the cozy corner of the couch while Papa knelt down at Bear’s eye level with a grin. “Tonight,” said Papa, “we are going to visit Steve in the Nether. He is trying to make an Eye of Ender, which is actually pretty simple if you have the right things. The trouble is… Steve is being so busy and so accidental that he keeps bonking into blazes, knocking blaze rods loose, and sort of defeating them without even meaning to.”

Bear blinked. “Oops.”

“Exactly,” said Papa. “And one of those blazes is friendly. So tonight, we have two jobs: help Steve get what he needs, and make very sure he does not accidentally mine our new blaze friend.”

Which way should the story begin? 1. Bear and Papa arrive at a Nether fortress bridge with smoke swirling everywhere. 2. Bear and Papa start in Steve’s funny little brewing room, where he is already mixing things up. 3. Bear and Papa meet the friendly blaze first, hiding behind a warm blackstone pillar.

Bear nodded. Papa smiled. “The funny little brewing room it is.”

Chapter 1

Bear and Papa stepped through the shimmer of a Nether portal and into a snug stone room tucked beside a Nether fortress wall. It was warmer than a kitchen and noisier than a workshop. Bottles clinked. Water burbled. A brewing stand fizzed with little puffs of sparkly steam.

And in the middle of it all was Steve.

Steve was wearing a puzzled look, three extra backpacks, and one boot that didn’t quite match the other.

“Hi, Steve,” said Bear.

“Hello,” said Steve, dropping a handful of blaze powder on the table, then scooping it back up with a spoon. “I am making an Eye of Ender.”

Papa looked around. “You seem to be making several other things too.”

Steve glanced left. There was a chest full of mushrooms. He glanced right. There was a cauldron with carrots in it for some reason. He looked up. A fishing rod was hanging from the ceiling.

“Yes,” Steve said. “That is true.”

Bear climbed onto a little stool to see better. “But an Eye of Ender is easy, right? It’s just an ender pearl and blaze powder.”

Steve froze. “...Oh.”

Papa’s eyes twinkled. “That’s all.”

Steve slowly set down a bundle labeled emergency beet soup ingredients. “So I may have done too much.”

“A little bit too much,” Bear said.

Steve nodded gravely. “I went to get blaze rods. Then I made a storage system. Then I organized the storage system. Then I forgot why I was organizing the storage system.”

That felt very Steve-ish.

Papa knelt to Bear’s eye level. “Let’s check what Steve already has.”

Bear loved that part. Checking things. Figuring out how they worked.

Together they searched the brewing room. In one chest they found glass bottles, redstone dust, string, a clock, and six wooden doors. In another chest they found one bright, swirly ender pearl.

“Good,” said Papa. “Half the job is already done.”

Steve perked up. “So I only need blaze powder.”

Just then, from outside the brewing room, there came a WHOOSH.

Then another.

WHOOSH. WHOOSH.

Steve blinked toward the doorway. “That sounds like blazes.”

Bear peeked out. The hallway beyond the room glowed orange from drifting firelight. Somewhere in the fortress, blazes were circling and crackling.

Then Steve reached for his pickaxe.

Papa gently put one paw on the handle. “Before we go anywhere, let’s make a plan.”

Bear pointed at the pickaxe. “Because you keep accidentally bonking blazes.”

Steve looked embarrassed. “I do sort of rush.”

“And,” Bear added, “one of the blazes is our friend, and we have to make sure nobody mines him by mistake.”

Steve straightened at once. “New plan. No accidental mining of friends.”

Papa looked pleased. “Excellent rule.”

Bear spotted a bit of chalk on a shelf and quickly drew a happy face on a flat shield hanging by the door. “What if friendly blazes need a sign?”

Steve gasped. “That is smart.”

Papa smiled. “So now we have a mission with two parts. Get blaze powder for the Eye of Ender. Keep the friendly blaze safe.”

Outside, the fortress gave another warm, rumbling WHOOSH.

Steve picked up the shield instead of the pickaxe.

“Much better,” said Papa.

Bear grinned. The Nether was hot and noisy and a little bit wild. But they had a job to do, and now they had a rule.

No accidental mining of friends.

### What should Bear do next? 1. Help Papa make a safe friend-sign so the friendly blaze can be recognized right away. 2. Go with Steve to the fortress hallway and peek carefully from behind the doorway first. 3. Search the brewing room for a clever tool that could help collect blaze rods without bonking anybody.

Bear rubbed his paws together. “Let’s do the clever tool.”

Chapter 2

The brewing room was messy in the way only Steve could make a room messy. Useful messy. Confusing messy. The kind of messy where a brilliant idea might be hiding under a fishing rod and two potatoes.

Bear opened a chest. “String.”

Papa looked into a barrel. “Iron nuggets.”

Steve lifted a flowerpot. “Button.”

Bear stared at him. “That is not for blaze rods.”

Steve set it down. “Right.”

Papa smiled and pointed to a workbench in the corner. “Let’s think about the problem first. We do not want to bonk anybody. We want the blaze rods to come to us safely, or at least let us collect them after a blaze is already gone.”

Bear loved that kind of thinking. Not just what to do. Why to do it.

“So,” said Bear, “we need a tool that helps from far away.”

“Exactly,” said Papa. “What works from far away?”

Bear looked around. “A fishing rod!”

Steve brightened. “I have one on the ceiling.”

He jumped, missed, found a stool, climbed up, and came back down with the rod in one hand and a dusty saddle in the other.

“Not the saddle,” said Bear.

“Yes,” said Steve, putting the saddle back on his head by accident. Then he took it off again. “Right.”

Papa laid the fishing rod on the table. “Now, a fishing rod will not pick up blaze rods by itself. But it can help us move something from a distance. Hook, pull, guide.”

Bear leaned in. “Like moving a basket!”

Papa’s eyes warmed. “That’s it.”

They searched again. In a lower chest, Bear found a small metal hopper. In another, Steve found two chains and announced, “I definitely remember why I kept these.”

Papa checked the parts. “Notice what each piece does. The hopper catches falling items. The chains let us lower it into a safer place. The fishing rod can tug it back.”

Bear nodded seriously. “Catch, lower, tug.”

Steve repeated, “Catch, lower, tug,” as if it were a very important scientific law.

Together they built a funny-looking tool: a little hanging catcher made from the hopper, chains, and a bent handle Steve made from spare iron. It looked a bit like a metal bucket trying very hard to be brave.

Bear gave it a test wiggle. Clink-clink.

Papa tied the fishing line through a ring on top. “Now, if we stay behind a wall or pillar, we may be able to lower this where blaze rods fall, then pull it back without charging into the middle of things.”

Bear’s blue eyes widened. “So the rods come to us.”

“Or close enough,” said Papa. “That is how tools help. We notice a problem, we ask how things work, and then we build something that uses those parts in a smart way.”

Steve held up the catcher. “I shall name it… the No-Bonk Basket.”

Bear laughed. “That is a perfect name.”

Then came a sudden WHOOOOSH from outside. A hotter one this time. Not wild. Not angry. More like a startled gasp made of fire.

Bear peeked through the doorway.

In the fortress hall beyond, orange sparks bobbed through the smoky air. Two blazes spun in slow circles near the far arch. And behind a dark blackstone pillar, just for a moment, Bear saw another blaze peek out.

This blaze did not charge.

He gave one tiny, worried wave.

Around one of his spinning rods was tied a crooked strip of teal cloth.

Bear whispered, “Papa… I think that’s our friend.”

Papa stepped beside Bear and lowered his voice too. “Good noticing.”

Steve gripped the No-Bonk Basket very carefully. “I will absolutely not bonk the one with the teal ribbon.”

The friendly blaze peeked out again, then quickly ducked back as the other blazes drifted closer, crackling and patrolling the corridor.

Bear could see the problem now. The friend blaze was hiding near the exact place Steve would probably rush into.

Which meant rushing was completely out.

Papa put one hand on Bear’s shoulder. “Now we choose the safest method.”

The hall was narrow. The pillars were tall. Heat shimmered in the air. Somewhere below, lava bubbled softly like soup in a giant pot.

Bear took a slow breath. This was exciting. A little tense too. But Papa was calm, Steve was listening, and the No-Bonk Basket clinked softly in Steve’s hands, ready to help.

### What should Bear choose? 1. Use the No-Bonk Basket from behind a pillar, and signal the friendly blaze to stay tucked safely back. 2. Ask the friendly blaze to quietly guide them through a side passage to a safer collecting spot. 3. Have Steve make the Eye of Ender ingredients list again out loud first, so he stays focused and careful before they go into the hall.

Bear carefully smoothed the piece of paper. “First, we keep Steve clear.”

Chapter 3

Papa set the paper on the brewing table, and Steve picked up a bit of charcoal like it was the most important pencil in the world.

“Now,” Papa said gently, “before we go into the hall, say the goal.”

Steve nodded, very serious now. He wrote in large blocky letters:

1. Ender pearl 2. Blaze powder 3. Make Eye of Ender 4. Do not bonk the friendly blaze

Bear added a little teal ribbon drawing beside number four.

“There,” said Bear. “Now it’s official.”

Steve read the list aloud once. Then again, slower. “Ender pearl. Blaze powder. Eye of Ender. No bonking friends.”

Papa smiled. “Good. When a job is simple, writing the steps can keep your brain from leaping into ten extra jobs.”

Bear pointed to the list. “So your brain doesn’t go, ‘Maybe I should build a ladder, organize a chest, bake potatoes, invent a machine—’”

“That,” said Steve, “is exactly what my brain does.”

Papa gave Bear a warm glance. “Sometimes the smartest thing is not doing more. It is keeping the pattern clear.”

Bear liked that. Keeping the pattern clear.

Steve tucked the paper into the front of his shirt so he could look at it anytime. Then he picked up the No-Bonk Basket, and together they stepped into the fortress hallway.

The Nether air wrapped around them at once, hot and dry and full of drifting gold sparks. The corridor stretched ahead between tall blackstone pillars. Orange light flickered across the floor. Far below, lava glowed through cracks in the fortress like a giant sleepy lantern.

WHOOSH.

Two regular blazes floated near the archway, circling lazily, rods spinning, eyes bright. They crackled and puffed little bursts of fire as they patrolled.

And just beyond them, tucked behind a pillar exactly where Bear had seen him before, was the friendly blaze.

The teal cloth around one rod fluttered.

He peeked out. Saw Bear. Saw Papa. Saw Steve.

Then he pointed urgently—not at himself, but at the floor near the arch.

Bear squinted. There, half-hidden in the smoky glow, lay two dropped blaze rods from some earlier scuffle in the fortress. Close enough to see. Not close enough to grab safely.

Steve took one big step forward.

Papa gently touched his arm. “List.”

Steve stopped. Blinked. Pulled the paper from his shirt. “Right. Blaze powder. Eye of Ender. No bonking friends.”

He took one big step backward again.

The friendly blaze gave a relieved little wiggle.

Bear whispered, “He’s helping us.”

Papa nodded. “And now we use the tool.”

They slipped behind a wide pillar where the regular blazes could not easily see them. Steve crouched. Papa steadied the chain. Bear held the bottom of the No-Bonk Basket so it would not clang against the stone too soon.

“Ready?” Papa asked.

Bear nodded.

Steve swallowed. “Ready.”

Papa leaned close to Bear’s ear. “Notice what we’re doing. We are using distance and shape. The basket does not fight. It collects. The chains lower it. The line pulls it back. Each part has a job.”

“Catch, lower, tug,” Bear whispered.

“Exactly.”

Steve lowered the No-Bonk Basket very, very slowly around the edge of the pillar.

Clink.

Too loud.

One of the regular blazes turned.

Everyone froze.

The blaze drifted nearer, rods spinning faster, eyes narrowing.

The friendly blaze popped halfway out from behind his pillar and made the tiniest cough-crackle sound, like, “Ahem!”

The regular blaze turned toward him instead.

The friendly blaze quickly bobbed toward a side alcove, pretending to inspect a wall torch very seriously.

The regular blaze followed, suspicious but distracted.

Bear let out the breath he had been holding. “He saved us.”

“He did,” Papa said softly. “Now we must help him back.”

Steve lowered the basket the rest of the way. The hopper settled right over the two blaze rods on the floor.

“Catch,” Bear whispered.

Steve gave the line a careful pull.

The basket tipped.

One rod slid in.

The other skittered away with a sharp clink and spun farther out into the corridor.

“Oh no,” Steve whispered.

WHOOSH!

The second regular blaze turned at the sound.

Its eyes flared. Fireballs began to glow around it.

Bear’s heart thumped fast.

Papa knelt quickly to Bear’s eye level even here, even now, calm in the hot glowing hall. “Fast heart is okay. One slow breath. In. Out. Good. Now we think.”

Bear breathed in. Breathed out. The thumping in his chest felt a little less jumpy.

“The loose rod,” Bear said. “It rolled into the open.”

“And the friendly blaze,” Papa said. “Still near the side alcove.”

Steve clutched the paper list with one hand and the fishing rod with the other. “I should not rush.”

“No,” said Bear. “But maybe our friend can help again.”

The friendly blaze peered around the alcove. Bear pointed to the loose rod, then to the basket, then made a rolling motion with his paws.

The friendly blaze’s eyes widened.

He nodded.

Very gently, with a soft puff of warm air from his spinning rods, he blew across the floor.

The loose blaze rod rolled.

Slowly at first. Then faster.

Straight toward the basket.

“Catch!” whispered Bear.

Steve dipped the No-Bonk Basket just in time.

Clink!

The second rod dropped neatly inside.

“Yes!” said Bear.

But the sound was enough. Both regular blazes turned at once.

WHOOSH-WHOOSH!

They spun up, bright and crackling, and a spray of fireballs lit the corridor.

“Behind the pillar,” said Papa.

They ducked back just as fizzing fire splashed against the stone. Sparks rained down and vanished.

Steve held the line tight. “The basket is full!”

“Then tug,” said Bear.

Steve tugged.

The chain caught on a broken brick.

“Oh, bother,” said Papa.

The regular blazes drifted closer, firing again. The pillar blocked most of it, but hot sparks snapped around the edges.

Bear looked from the stuck chain, to Steve, to the paper list peeking out of his shirt, to the friendly blaze waiting in the alcove.

Think.

Not rush.

Think.

“Papa,” Bear said, “if the basket is stuck, we don’t need to pull harder. We need to change the angle.”

Papa’s face brightened. “Yes.”

Bear pointed upward. “If Steve lifts the rod tip high, and you guide the chain from low, it might pop free.”

“Like changing the direction of the force,” Papa said.

Steve blinked. “That sounds extremely smart.”

“It is also true,” said Papa.

The regular blazes surged around the far side of the pillar.

The friendly blaze zipped out from the alcove and spun in a quick circle right in front of them, drawing their attention with a showy burst of harmless sparks.

“Now!” Bear cried.

Steve lifted the fishing rod high.

Papa guided the lower chain sideways.

Bear grabbed the loose middle link and gave it one firm little nudge.

Pop!

The chain sprang free.

The No-Bonk Basket swung back around the pillar and into Steve’s arms, both blaze rods safely inside.

The friendly blaze darted back behind the pillar beside them just as the regular blazes fired one last confused blast down the corridor and drifted off, grumbling in crackly little whooshes.

For a moment, everyone was still.

Then Bear looked into the basket.

Two blaze rods.

Safe.

Collected.

No bonking.

Steve stared at them as if they were treasure. “We did it.”

The friendly blaze floated lower, a little nervous now that things were quiet. Up close, his glow was softer than the others’. More golden than sharp. The teal ribbon around one rod was slightly singed at the ends.

Bear smiled at him. “Hi.”

The blaze gave a shy little wave.

Papa bowed his head politely. “Thank you for your help.”

Steve carefully showed him the list. Then he pointed to number four: Do not bonk the friendly blaze.

The blaze looked at it. Then at Steve.

Then he made a delighted fizzing sound, like a campfire laughing.

Bear giggled. “I think he likes being on the list.”

Steve put his hand over his heart. “You are officially protected by my checklist.”

The friendly blaze twirled once, proud as could be.

Papa looked down the corridor toward the brewing room. “We have the rods. The hardest part is done.”

Bear nodded. His legs felt a little shaky now that the exciting part was over, but in a good way. They had noticed how the tool worked. They had kept the steps clear. And when things got tricky, they had changed the angle instead of just yanking harder.

That felt clever.

And kind.

### What should happen next? 1. Bring the friendly blaze back to the brewing room doorway, where it is safer while Steve makes the blaze powder. 2. Ask the friendly blaze to show them a quiet shortcut home, so they can avoid the patrol blazes on the way back. 3. Have Bear carry the checklist and be the official step-reader, making sure Steve follows each part calmly on the return trip.

Bear grinned. “Torch inspectors.”

Steve gasped softly. “That is brilliant.”

Chapter 4

Papa’s eyes sparkled. “A calm disguise. I like it.”

The friendly blaze floated a little higher, curious.

Bear pointed at the wall torches along the corridor. “The regular blazes are already drifting around here. If we act like we belong here, and we stop at each torch like we’re checking it, then nobody will think we’re sneaking.”

Steve stood straighter at once. “Yes. We are professionals.”

Papa folded the checklist neatly and handed it to Bear. “Then Bear can be the lead inspector.”

Bear took it proudly. “I can read the jobs.”

The friendly blaze gave a cheerful fizz and bobbed beside a wall torch, pretending to study it with great seriousness.

“That,” said Steve, “is excellent inspecting.”

Papa smiled. “Let’s make the disguise simple. Simple is easier to remember.”

So they made their plan right there behind the pillar.

Bear would carry the checklist and say each step in a steady voice. Papa would carry the No-Bonk Basket with the blaze rods tucked safely inside. Steve would walk slowly and not grab random tools. The friendly blaze would drift ahead and pause by each torch as though he were part of the inspection team.

“And what do inspectors do?” Papa asked.

Bear answered right away. “They look carefully. They don’t rush.”

Steve nodded. “Very inspector-like.”

They stepped out from behind the pillar.

At once, the hallway felt big again. The blackstone arches stretched long and shadowy. Heat shimmered in the air. Lava glowed down through the cracks below. The wall torches burned with steady orange flames, each one hissing quietly against the dark stone.

The friendly blaze floated to the first torch and gave it a tiny approving circle.

Bear cleared his throat and read from the checklist in his best official voice. “Step one: carry the blaze rods safely. Step two: no bonking friends. Step three: walk calmly.”

Steve folded his hands behind his back. “Inspection posture.”

Papa nearly chuckled, but kept his face serious. “Very convincing.”

They stopped at the first torch.

Bear leaned in. “Looks glowy.”

Papa nodded thoughtfully. “Excellent torch behavior.”

Steve squinted at it. “Yes. Definitely torch.”

The friendly blaze gave a tiny pleased whoosh and moved on to the next one.

They followed.

Down the corridor they went, stopping at every torch. Look. Nod. Walk. Look. Nod. Walk.

At the far end of the hall, one of the regular blazes drifted into view.

Bear’s tummy gave a little jump.

The regular blaze hovered near an arch, rods spinning slowly. Its bright eyes turned toward them.

Papa lowered his voice without sounding worried. “Slow breath.”

Bear breathed in. Breathed out.

Then the friendly blaze zipped to the nearest wall torch and spun around it in a neat little circle, like the busiest torch inspector in the Nether.

Bear lifted the paper and read, “Torch number… um… seven. Very fiery. Good work, torch.”

Steve added, “Passed inspection.”

Papa gave a solemn nod.

The regular blaze watched them for a moment.

Then, to Bear’s amazement, it drifted over to another torch a few blocks away and stared at it too, as if perhaps checking torches was simply what everyone was doing now.

Bear had to bite his lip to keep from laughing.

They moved to the next torch.

And the next.

At each one, the friendly blaze paused. Bear read the list. Steve stayed focused. Papa carried the basket steady and low so nothing clinked.

It was working.

Then, just when the brewing room doorway came into sight at the end of the corridor, they reached a torch that was sputtering weakly.

Pffft. Pffft.

Its flame shrank to a tiny orange bead.

Then it went out.

The corridor dimmed on one side at once.

The regular blaze behind them gave a puzzled WHOOSH.

Steve’s eyes widened. “A failed torch inspection!”

Papa bent to Bear’s eye level. “One last small problem.”

Bear looked at the dark torch. The brewing room was close. The regular blaze was curious now. And the friendly blaze was already hovering by the empty bracket, looking worried.

Then Bear noticed something.

The torch wasn’t broken-broken. It was loose. Tilted. The tip had slipped out of the hottest part of its little netherrack cradle.

“It just needs to be put back in the right place,” Bear said.

Papa smiled. “You noticed the position.”

Steve brightened. “So we do not need a whole new torch. We just adjust it.”

Bear nodded. “Like changing the angle of the chain.”

The friendly blaze puffed a careful, warm breath toward the torch head while Papa reached up and gently nudged the base straighter with the handle of the No-Bonk Basket.

For one tiny second, nothing happened.

Then—

FWOOM.

The torch lit again, steady and bright.

The regular blaze gave an approving crackle, as if satisfied that the inspection team had done excellent work, and drifted away down the side passage.

Bear let out a happy laugh. “We really looked like inspectors.”

Steve put a hand over his chest. “I have never been so professional.”

Papa’s eyes twinkled. “And now we are safely back.”

They slipped through the brewing room doorway together. The stone room felt extra cozy after the hot corridor outside. Bottles clinked softly. The brewing stand bubbled. The basket sat on the table, and inside it the two blaze rods glowed faintly like warm little sticks of sunset.

The friendly blaze hovered just inside the doorway, safe at last.

Bear looked up at Papa. “Now we make the powder.”

Papa nodded. “And then the Eye of Ender.”

Steve pulled the checklist out once more and smoothed it flat on the table. He looked calmer now. Clearer. He had what he needed, and he had not gotten swept away by ten extra jobs.

That felt like a victory too.

Chapter 5

Back in the warm little brewing room, the exciting part was over at last. The bottles clinked softly. The brewing stand gave a gentle blurp-blurp. Steve set the blaze rods on the table very carefully, as if they were made of sunshine.

Bear found a piece of wood, a bit of paper, and a charcoal stub. Papa knelt beside him at eye level. “What should the sign say?”

Bear thought for a moment, then wrote in neat, careful letters:

THANK YOU, FRIENDLY BLAZE OFFICIAL HELPER DO NOT BONK

The friendly blaze floated closer and read it. His glow turned warm and bright, and he made a happy fizzing sound like a tiny fire laughing.

Steve put one hand over his heart. “That is an excellent sign.”

Papa smiled. “Clear, kind, and useful.”

Bear taped a little teal ribbon drawing in the corner, just like the one the friendly blaze wore. Then Steve hung the sign beside the brewing stand where everyone could see it.

“Now,” said Bear, picking up the checklist, “step by step.”

Steve nodded and read with him. “Ender pearl. Blaze powder. Make Eye of Ender.”

He ground one blaze rod into powder. No extra jobs. No random storage system. No beet soup. Just the one simple recipe.

Papa watched with a pleased look. “You stayed with the pattern.”

Steve mixed the blaze powder with the ender pearl.

Pop.

There it was.

A shining Eye of Ender rested in Steve’s square hand.

“We did it,” Bear said softly.

“And,” Papa added, “we did it safely.”

The friendly blaze gave one proud twirl beneath his sign.

Soon it was time to go. Bear and Papa waved goodbye at the brewing room door. Steve waved too, holding the Eye of Ender in one hand and glancing at the thank-you sign with a grin.

Back home, everything felt cool and quiet after the warm Nether glow. Bear snuggled in close, sleepy now.

Papa tucked the blanket gently around him. “Tonight we remembered that simple steps can help a busy brain, and kind plans can protect a friend.”

Bear smiled drowsily. “And torch inspectors are very professional.”

Papa chuckled softly. “They certainly are.”

The house was calm. Bear was safe. And before long, he drifted into sleep.
