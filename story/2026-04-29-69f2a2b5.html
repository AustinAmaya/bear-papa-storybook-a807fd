Bear and Papa and the Robot Cheetah

The night was soft and blue over Papa Bear Home.

Preamble

The night was soft and blue over Papa Bear Home.

Bear was in pajamas with little rockets on them. Papa was reading a book called *How Stars Twinkle Without Trying Very Hard.*

Then came a sound from the deck.

Tap tap tap. ZOOM. Tap tap tap. ZOOM.

Little Red Bear, who was happily retired on the deck, gave a tiny statue wobble.

Little Polar Bear, watching from the fence post, pointed one paw at the sky.

A silver streak landed in the yard.

It had four springy legs, bright green eyes, and a tail like an antenna.

“Hello,” it said. “I am Cheetah. I am a robot cheetah. I require two clever bears.”

Bear sat up very straight.

Papa closed the book.

“Well,” Papa said, “we are bears. And one of us is clever enough for both of us.”

Bear grinned. “That’s me.”

“Obviously,” said Papa.

Chapter 1: The Call from Space

Cheetah’s metal paws clicked on the porch.

“I come from the International Space Station,” said Cheetah. “But not the human one.”

Bear blinked. “There is another one?”

“Yes,” said Cheetah. “The Robot International Space Station. RISS. It is like the ISS, but with more blinking lights and fewer socks floating away.”

Papa knelt to Bear’s eye level. “Pressurized modules are rooms in space that hold air inside, so astronauts—or robots visiting with air-loving friends—can be comfortable.”

Cheetah nodded so fast his ears made a soft *whirr.*

“The modules are pressurized,” he said. “But the oxygen system is not working. The robot crew does not breathe, so they did not notice at first.”

Bear gasped. “But if bears visit—”

“No oxygen,” said Cheetah. “No visit. No repair. No bedtime snack.”

“That last part is serious,” Papa said.

Cheetah opened a little hatch in his side. Inside were two space helmets, folded like shiny balloons.

“I can carry you there. I am extremely fast.”

“How fast?” Bear asked.

Cheetah crouched.

The yard lights flickered.

Little Polar Bear held onto the fence with both paws.

“Space-fast,” said Cheetah.

Papa packed a tiny tool roll, two apple slices, and Bear’s favorite small screwdriver.

Bear zipped his jacket.

“Ready,” Bear said.

Cheetah lowered his back. “Climb aboard. Please keep paws and jokes inside the robot cheetah at all times.”

Papa climbed on behind Bear.

“Three,” said Cheetah.

“Two,” said Bear.

“One,” said Papa.

Cheetah sprang.

The house became small. The clouds became silver. The moon looked like a sleepy cookie.

Bear held Papa’s paw.

Papa said, “Breathe slow. In through your nose. Out like cooling soup.”

Bear breathed.

The stars came closer.

And ahead, floating bright and round, was the Robot International Space Station.

Chapter 2: The Robot Crew

Cheetah docked with a gentle *clonk.*

“Welcome to RISS,” said a round robot with a screen-face. “I am Captain Bolts.”

Another robot floated upside down. It had six arms and a voice like a kazoo.

“I am Wrenchley. I organize tools by shininess.”

A tiny square robot bumped into Bear’s boot.

“I am Beep,” it said. “I am small but emotionally significant.”

Bear giggled.

Papa checked the air gauge on the wall. The needle sat in the red.

“Oxygen is very low,” Papa said. “Our suits have tanks, but the station needs its own air.”

Captain Bolts spun sadly in place. “We tried pressing the big blue button.”

“What happened?” Bear asked.

“It blinked.”

“And then?”

“It continued blinking.”

Papa rubbed his chin. “Then the button is probably not the problem.”

Bear looked around. Pipes ran along the wall. Some were blue. Some were white. One had a sticker of a carrot on it.

“Why does that pipe have a carrot?” Bear asked.

Wrenchley waved three arms. “That is not oxygen. That is snack vapor.”

Papa smiled. “Good noticing. Systems often use colors and labels. We follow the oxygen line.”

They floated down the module, hand over hand.

Bear liked floating. His feet kept forgetting to be feet.

Cheetah padded along the wall with magnetic paws.

At the oxygen machine, a red light flashed:

O2 GENERATOR: WATER INPUT LOW. FILTER BLOCKED.

Bear read it slowly. “It makes oxygen from water?”

“It can,” Papa said. “Some space systems split water into hydrogen and oxygen using electricity. Like taking H-two-O apart.”

Bear’s eyes went wide. “Water is made of parts?”

“Tiny parts,” said Papa. “Too tiny to see. But machines can still work with them.”

Beep bumped the wall. “I also am made of parts. Some are embarrassing.”

Captain Bolts opened a panel.

Inside was a filter packed with gray space dust.

“There is our first problem,” Papa said.

Bear held up his little screwdriver. “Can I?”

Papa nodded. “Carefully. Slow is smooth.”

Bear loosened the screws. Wrenchley caught each one with a little net.

The filter slid out with a dusty *fwump.*

Cheetah sneezed sparks. “Apologies. Simulated allergy.”

They cleaned the filter and put it back.

The machine hummed.

Then it coughed.

Then it stopped.

A second warning blinked:

WATER LINE JAMMED. INSPECTION REQUIRED.

Captain Bolts’ screen-face turned into a worried square.

Bear swallowed. “We fixed one problem. But not the whole problem.”

Papa put a paw on his shoulder.

“That is engineering,” Papa said. “You learn one clue at a time.”

Chapter 3: The Oxygen Fix

The water line ran through a narrow tunnel between modules.

It was too small for Papa.

Too twisty for Captain Bolts.

Too delicate for Wrenchley.

Beep tried to look brave. “I am small. But I turn left when I mean right.”

Bear looked at Cheetah.

Cheetah’s green eyes blinked. “I can run through the outside maintenance rail. But someone must guide me from inside.”

Bear raised his paw. “I can do it.”

Papa looked at him carefully. “You can. And we will do it together.”

Bear wore a little headset. Papa floated beside the control panel. Cheetah stepped into the airlock.

“Airlock cycling,” said Captain Bolts.

The door opened to black, sparkly space.

Cheetah launched outside with magnetic paws clicking along the station’s skin.

Bear watched a screen that showed the pipe map.

“Go forward,” Bear said. “Now left. Not snack-vapor left. Oxygen-water left.”

“Excellent clarification,” said Cheetah.

Papa pointed to a pulsing dot. “That is the jam.”

Bear zoomed the camera.

Something glittery was stuck in the water valve.

It looked like a tiny crown.

Wrenchley gasped. “My ceremonial bolt washer!”

“You have a ceremonial bolt washer?” Bear asked.

“For formal repairs,” Wrenchley said.

The washer had slipped into the water line and blocked the flow.

Cheetah reached the outside hatch.

“I can open the service cover,” he said. “But the washer is inside a valve. I cannot grab it.”

Bear studied the diagram.

“What if we push water gently from the other side?” he asked.

Papa’s eyes warmed. “Backflush. Very good.”

“Like when a straw has a blueberry stuck,” Bear said. “You blow the other way.”

“Exactly,” Papa said.

Captain Bolts rolled to the water tank controls. “Ready.”

Papa held up one claw. “Gentle pressure. Too much could shake the pipe.”

Bear watched the gauge.

“Little more,” he said.

The needle rose.

“Little more.”

The pipe trembled.

Bear’s tummy trembled too.

Papa said softly, “Name what you see.”

“Gauge at yellow,” Bear said.

“Name what you feel.”

“Nervous.”

“Good. Nervous is allowed. What do we do?”

“We go slow,” Bear said.

The washer wiggled on the screen.

Cheetah braced his magnetic paws. “I see sparkle movement.”

“Pulse it,” Bear said. “Not one big push. Tiny pushes.”

Captain Bolts tapped the control.

*Pip.*

The washer wiggled.

*Pip.*

It turned sideways.

*Pip.*

It shot free.

Cheetah caught it in his tail magnet.

“Ceremonial object secured,” he said.

Wrenchley clapped all six hands. “My formal repairs are saved!”

The water line filled. The oxygen machine hummed again.

Blue bubbles danced in a clear tube.

The station lights shifted from red to gold.

O2 GENERATOR ACTIVE. OXYGEN RISING.

Bear bounced in the air.

“We did it!”

Papa caught him gently. “You noticed, asked how it worked, and used the idea.”

Beep bumped Bear’s knee. “I am emotionally significant and also impressed.”

But then the station gave one more beep.

Not a scary beep.

A fussy beep.

VENTILATION FANS OFFLINE. OXYGEN NOT MIXING.

Captain Bolts’ face became three dots.

Papa sighed. “The oxygen is being made, but it is staying near the machine. We need to move it through the rooms.”

Bear looked at the fans along the walls.

“Like stirring soup,” he said.

“Exactly,” Papa said.

Chapter 4: Stirring the Air

The fan control panel was at the far end of the module.

Unfortunately, the far end of the module was full of floating things.

A wrench.

A sock.

A spoon.

A rubber duck in a tiny space helmet.

Captain Bolts cleared his throat. “The crew has been meaning to tidy.”

Cheetah crouched. “I can retrieve objects at high speed.”

“No high speed indoors,” Papa said quickly.

Cheetah froze. “Medium speed?”

“Library speed,” Bear said.

Cheetah looked horrified. “I will try.”

They made a chain.

Wrenchley gathered tools. Beep gathered small things. Cheetah nudged the rubber duck with great dignity.

Bear floated to the fan panel.

The switches were labeled:

FAN A. FAN B. FAN C. DO NOT PRESS ALL AT ONCE UNLESS YOU ENJOY CHAOS.

Bear read the labels twice.

“Papa?”

“Yes?”

“Why not all at once?”

“Starting motors can use a lot of power. One at a time is safer.”

Bear clicked Fan A.

A soft breeze moved his ears.

He clicked Fan B.

The rubber duck saluted.

He clicked Fan C.

Air began to flow through the station.

The oxygen gauge moved slowly upward.

Red to yellow.

Yellow to green.

Captain Bolts’ screen became a big smile.

“Pressurized modules restored for oxygen-breathing guests,” he said.

Wrenchley bowed. “Please accept this non-ceremonial sticker.”

He placed a shiny sticker on Bear’s sleeve. It said:

JUNIOR AIR SYSTEM ENGINEER

Beep bumped Papa gently. “Papa also helped.”

Wrenchley added a second sticker:

SENIOR CALM VOICE ENGINEER

Papa looked at it. “A very respectable title.”

Cheetah opened his side hatch. “Return transport is ready.”

Bear looked once more at Earth through the round window.

It was blue and white and quiet.

“Papa,” he whispered, “we were really in space.”

Papa put an arm around him.

“Yes,” he said. “And space was lucky you came.”

Chapter 5: Home Again

Cheetah carried them home through the dark.

This time Bear was not scared.

He watched the stars slide by like tiny night-lights.

Papa’s paw stayed close, warm and steady.

They landed softly in the yard.

Little Polar Bear gave a proud statue nod from the fence post.

Little Red Bear stayed retired, but somehow looked pleased.

Cheetah bowed.

“Mission complete,” he said. “Oxygen restored. Robots grateful. Rubber duck accounted for.”

Bear yawned. “That was important.”

“It was,” Papa said. “And you solved it the way good engineers do. You noticed the clues. You asked how the system worked. Then you tried careful fixes.”

Bear touched the sticker on his sleeve.

“Water can turn into oxygen,” he murmured.

“With the right machine,” Papa said.

“And air needs stirring.”

“Just like soup.”

Inside, Papa tucked Bear into bed. The rocket pajamas were wrinkled from space travel. The sticker shone softly on the blanket.

“Papa?”

“Yes, Bear?”

“Do robots sleep?”

“Some recharge,” Papa said. “Which is robot sleeping with extra beeps.”

Bear smiled.

Papa turned off the lamp.

The room became calm and blue again.

Outside, the two little statues watched over the quiet neighborhood.

Inside, Bear breathed easy.

In.

Out.

Like cooling soup.

And soon he was asleep.

“Like when a straw has a blueberry stuck,” Bear said. “You blow the other way.”

“Exactly.”

Captain Bolts rolled to the water controls.

Papa held up one claw. “Gentle pressure.”

Bear watched the gauge.

“Little more,” he said.

The pipe trembled. Bear’s tummy trembled too.

Papa said softly, “Name what you see.”

“Gauge at yellow.”

“Name what you feel.”

“Nervous.”

“Good. Nervous is allowed. What do we do?”

“We go slow,” Bear said.

The washer wiggled.

“Pulse it,” Bear said. “Tiny pushes.”

Captain Bolts tapped the control.

Pip.

The washer turned.

Pip.

It slid.

Pip.

It shot free.

Cheetah caught it with his tail magnet.

“Ceremonial object secured.”

The water line filled. The oxygen machine hummed.

Blue bubbles danced in a clear tube.

O2 GENERATOR ACTIVE. OXYGEN RISING.

Bear bounced. “We did it!”

But the station gave one more fussy beep.

VENTILATION FANS OFFLINE. OXYGEN NOT MIXING.

Papa nodded. “The oxygen is being made, but it needs to move through the rooms.”

Bear looked at the fans. “Like stirring soup?”

“Exactly,” Papa said.

Chapter 4: Stirring the Air

The fan panel was at the far end of the module.

Unfortunately, the way was full of floating things.

A wrench. A spoon. A sock. A rubber duck in a tiny space helmet.

Captain Bolts cleared his throat. “We have been meaning to tidy.”

Cheetah crouched. “I can retrieve objects at high speed.”

“No high speed indoors,” Papa said.

“Medium speed?”

“Library speed,” Bear said.

Cheetah looked horrified. “I will try.”

They made a chain. Wrenchley gathered tools. Beep gathered small things. Cheetah nudged the rubber duck with great dignity.

Bear floated to the fan panel.

The switches read:

FAN A. FAN B. FAN C. DO NOT PRESS ALL AT ONCE UNLESS YOU ENJOY CHAOS.

“Why not all at once?” Bear asked.

“Motors use lots of power when they start,” Papa said. “One at a time is safer.”

Bear clicked Fan A.

A soft breeze moved his ears.

He clicked Fan B.

The rubber duck saluted.

He clicked Fan C.

Air flowed through the station.

The oxygen gauge moved from red to yellow.

Then yellow to green.

Captain Bolts’ screen became a big smile.

“Pressurized modules restored for oxygen-breathing guests.”

Wrenchley bowed and placed a shiny sticker on Bear’s sleeve.

JUNIOR AIR SYSTEM ENGINEER

Beep bumped Papa gently. “Papa also helped.”

Wrenchley added a second sticker.

SENIOR CALM VOICE ENGINEER

Papa looked proud. “A very respectable title.”

Chapter 5: Home Again

Cheetah carried them home through the dark.

This time Bear was not scared. The stars slid by like tiny night-lights. Papa’s paw stayed close and warm.

They landed softly in the yard.

Little Polar Bear gave a proud statue nod. Little Red Bear stayed retired, but somehow looked pleased.

Cheetah bowed.

“Mission complete. Oxygen restored. Robots grateful. Rubber duck accounted for.”

Bear yawned. “That was important.”

“It was,” Papa said. “You noticed clues. You asked how the system worked. Then you tried careful fixes.”

Bear touched the sticker on his sleeve.

“Water can help make oxygen,” he murmured.

“With the right machine,” Papa said.

“And air needs stirring.”

“Just like soup.”

Inside, Papa tucked Bear into bed. The rocket pajamas were wrinkled from space travel. The sticker shone softly on the blanket.

“Papa?”

“Yes, Bear?”

“Do robots sleep?”

“Some recharge,” Papa said. “Which is robot sleeping with extra beeps.”

Bear smiled.

Papa turned off the lamp.

The room became calm and blue.

Outside, the two little statues watched over the quiet neighborhood.

Inside, Bear breathed easy.

In.

Out.

Like cooling soup.

And soon he was asleep.
