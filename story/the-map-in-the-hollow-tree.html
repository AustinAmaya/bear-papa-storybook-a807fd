The Map in the Hollow Tree

---

---

Preamble

Papa had an idea.

"I want to look at the Great Old Tree again," he said. "More carefully this time."

Bear was at the kitchen table, looking at the map in his blue notebook. He'd been adding to it — he'd drawn the path to the Quiet Creek, and the shape of the Bowl's rim, and a small star above the Observation Stone clearing to mark where the Shepherd Star appeared. He wrote: *The map is not finished.* And that was still true.

"Why more carefully?" Bear asked.

Papa was putting on his walking coat. "A tree that old has had a lot of time. And old things sometimes hold things."

"Like what?"

Papa thought about it. "I don't know yet," he said honestly. "That's why I want to look."

Bear closed the notebook and put it in his vest pocket — he had a vest with a good pocket just the right size for the notebook. He stood up.

"I'm coming," Bear said.

"I know," Papa said.

---

Chapter 1

The new path was familiar now.

Bear could feel it. The first time they'd come this way it had felt strange and uncertain. Now his feet knew the curve. They knew the softer ground. They knew where to expect the root that crossed the path like a speed bump.

Bear pointed to it without looking. "Root," he said.

Papa stepped over it. "Good."

The morning was cool and the woods smelled like soil and pine. A bird was making a sound high up — not the same bird as before, Bear thought. Or maybe it was. He didn't know bird sounds well enough yet.

*That's something to learn*, he thought.

He wrote it in his notebook while walking: *Learn bird sounds.*

Papa smiled without looking at him. Somehow Papa always knew when Bear was writing things down.

They came to the Great Old Tree.

It was just as big as before. That was the thing about a tree this size — you expected it to have changed somehow, grown, shifted. But no. It was exactly the same. Patient and enormous.

Papa walked all the way around it.

Bear hadn't done that before. He followed.

The other side of the Great Old Tree was different.

---

Chapter 2

The far side of the Great Old Tree had a hollow.

Not just a small hollow — not like the root hollow in the Bowl. This was a proper hollow, starting about waist-height on Bear, going up about as tall as his arm span. Wide enough to step inside. The wood at the edges was old and smooth, worn by years and weather. Inside, the smell was dark and dry.

Bear looked in carefully.

"There's something in there," he said.

Papa bent down and looked in beside him. He reached in slowly, careful about the edges.

He pulled something out.

It was flat, roughly rectangular. Wrapped in something that had once been oilskin — that waxed cloth that kept water out. The waxed cloth was brown and crumbly at the edges, very old, but it had done its job. Whatever was inside was dry.

Papa and Bear looked at each other.

Papa unwrapped it slowly, on his knee.

Inside was paper. Brownish, fragile. But holding together.

On the paper: lines. And marks. And symbols.

Bear's breath stopped.

"Papa," he whispered. "That's a *map*."

---

Chapter 3

They brought it back to the kitchen table.

Papa put a cloth down first, then set the old map on the cloth. He got a magnifying glass from his study — the good one, with the wooden handle. Bear pulled a chair right next to Papa and they both leaned in.

The map showed the Whispering Woods. Bear could tell because of the shape of the treeline. He could see the edge of something that might be the yard — now their yard. And paths. Lots of paths.

"These are different paths," Bear said. "More than we know."

"Much more," Papa said. "Whoever made this spent a lot of time in these woods."

Bear pointed to a mark near the center of the map. "What's that?"

Papa looked closely. The mark was a circle with a line through it — slightly different from the carved circle on the Observation Stone, but similar. Near it was a small dot, and from the dot, a short line pointing up.

"I think," Papa said slowly, "that might be the Observation Stone."

Bear looked at his blue notebook map, then at the old map. He compared them.

"If that's the Observation Stone," Bear said, tracing with his finger without touching the paper, "then... *this* is the Bowl. And this path that curves here — is the new path."

"Yes," Papa said. "I think so."

"So this person *knew* the Bowl too."

"Before we did."

Bear sat back. The kitchen was very quiet.

"Papa. Who made this map?"

---

Chapter 4

Papa went to his study and came back with a folder. Inside was a photograph of a star chart — he'd had it for a long time, Bear knew. The chart showed the Shepherd Star, and next to it, in careful, small handwriting: *4 hr anomalous brightening. 1923. Cause unknown.*

Papa set the star chart photograph next to the old map.

He looked at both of them for a long time.

Bear waited. He knew when Papa was thinking.

"The handwriting," Papa said finally, "looks the same."

Bear looked at the old map's labels. There were a few, in the corners, marking directions. And next to the circle-mark: a tiny word. He squinted.

"What does it say?"

Papa picked up the magnifying glass. Read. Set it down.

"Stone," Papa said. "Just the word: *Stone.*"

Bear looked at the map. The old map, marked with the places they'd found — the Bowl, the Observation Stone, paths they'd only just walked. And now: the same handwriting as the 1923 star chart.

"The person who watched the Shepherd Star in 1923," Bear said carefully, "also made this map. And they hid it in the Great Old Tree."

"Yes," Papa said. "I think so."

"And they built the Observation Stone."

"Or found it."

Bear looked at the map. "Do you think they lived near here?"

Papa was quiet for a moment. "I think they loved these woods," he said. "The same way we do."

---

Chapter 5

Papa photographed the old map with his camera, very carefully, so they wouldn't have to handle it more than necessary. Then he wrapped it back in fresh cloth and put it in the wide flat drawer of his desk where he kept things he wanted to protect.

Bear copied the map into his blue notebook — not every detail, but the shape, the paths, the circle mark for the Observation Stone, the dot that might be the Bowl.

He held up both pages: his map and the copied old one.

Same woods. Different times.

"Papa," Bear said, "we should tell Cheetah."

"She'll want to come back immediately," Papa said.

"She'll *sprint* back."

Papa smiled. "Yes, she will."

Bear looked at the old map copy in his notebook. He thought about someone in these woods a hundred years ago, walking the same paths, looking up at the same star, hiding a map in a hollow tree.

Maybe they'd thought: *Someday someone will find this.*

And Bear had.

Little Red Bear spoke from the railing when they went back outside: "Did you find something important?"

"Yes," Bear said. "I think we found a friend. From a long time ago."

Little Red Bear was quiet for a moment.

"That's the best kind of finding," Little Red Bear said.

Bear sat on the deck step and looked at the Whispering Woods. The trees whispered. The evening light came gold through the leaves.

*We'll figure it out*, Bear thought. *Someday we'll know who you were.*

*The End.*
