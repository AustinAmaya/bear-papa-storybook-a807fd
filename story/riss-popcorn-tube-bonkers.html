The Popcorn Tube That Went Bonkers

Bear and Papa help the RISS crew calm a popcorn tube that is firing tiny delicious asteroids everywhere.

Chapter 1 — The Yellow Button

Papa Bear Home was quiet in the late afternoon. The deck boards were warm from the sun. Little Red Bear sat in his usual place, still and shiny and red, watching the yard without moving at all.

Bear was on the deck with Papa, building a tiny spaceship out of three blocks, two bottle caps, and one very important rubber band.

"This part is the engine," Bear said.

"Excellent," Papa said. "What does the rubber band do?"

Bear stretched it carefully. "Emergency bounce power."

Papa nodded. "Every serious spacecraft needs emergency bounce power."

Just then, a soft beep came from Papa's tablet.

Not a regular beep.

A RISS beep.

The screen lit up with Captain Bolts's round robot face. His screen-mouth was shaped like a worried rectangle.

"HELLO, FRIENDS," Captain Bolts said. "THIS IS NOT AN EMERGENCY."

Behind him, something white flew past.

Then another.

Then twelve more.

Captain Bolts blinked. "IT IS A SNACK-BASED URGENCY."

Commander Aero appeared in a smaller window beside him, calm as always, her flight jacket zipped neatly and her eyes focused.

"Hello, Bear. Hello, Papa," she said. "The Robot International Space Station has a popcorn tube malfunction. The tube is supposed to dispense popped popcorn into snack bowls. Instead, it is firing popcorn into the station like tiny delicious asteroids."

On screen, Beep drifted sideways with one piece of popcorn stuck to its square robot head.

Beep made two proud beeps. Its screen-face flashed a tiny picture of a medal.

Bear sat up straight. "Can we help?"

Captain Bolts pressed something off screen. A giant blue button made a dramatic clonking sound.

"PLEASE YES," he said.

Papa looked at Bear. He did not rush. He put one hand lightly on Bear's shoulder.

"Space station rules?" Papa asked.

Bear held up one finger. "Slow hands."

Another finger. "Clip everything."

A third finger. "Don't chase floaty snacks."

"Perfect," Papa said.

Commander Aero smiled. "Tablet bridge ready. RISS docking path is clear."

The silver light opened in the air above the deck. It hummed softly, like a refrigerator that knew a secret.

Bear tucked the rubber band into his pocket. "Emergency bounce power," he whispered.

Papa clipped his tool pouch to his belt. "Just in case the popcorn is very advanced."

Little Red Bear stayed exactly still, but a tiny folded note rested against his base. Bear picked it up.

It said, in small neat letters: Popcorn is easier to catch when you know where it wants to go.

Bear grinned. "Good point."

Then he and Papa stepped through the tablet bridge, out of the warm deck light and into the bright, humming halls of the RISS.

Popcorn immediately bounced gently off Papa's forehead.

Papa blinked.

Bear giggled.

Captain Bolts floated toward them with both robot arms spread wide. "WELCOME ABOARD," he said. "PLEASE ENJOY OUR MANY, MANY SNACKS."

Chapter 2 — Tiny Delicious Asteroids

The RISS hallway looked like a snow globe someone had filled with popcorn and then shaken very politely.

Popcorn drifted past the lights. Popcorn bumped softly against handrails. Popcorn collected in corners where the air fans made little swirls. One piece spun slowly in front of Bear's nose like it was trying to hypnotize him.

Bear did not grab it.

He remembered the rule.

Slow hands.

Papa clipped both of their safety lines to the yellow rail. "First we look," he said. "Then we understand. Then we fix."

Beep made a hopeful little beep and pointed one square corner toward the popcorn.

"Maybe after we understand," Papa said.

Beep's screen-face showed deep thinking. It beeped slowly, then displayed a tiny snack bowl after a tiny light bulb.

Captain Bolts led them to the snack module. The popcorn tube ran along the ceiling in a clear pipe, marked with a cheerful sticker that said POP! in orange letters. Every few seconds, the tube made a thump-whirr-paff sound.

Paff!

A puff of popcorn shot out of the dispenser and scattered across the room.

Wrenchley, the six-armed robot, was trying to catch it with six different tools at once: a net, a cup, a spoon, a soft grabber, a shiny tray, and what appeared to be a tiny flag.

"I am organizing the popcorn by shininess!" Wrenchley announced.

"Popcorn is not very shiny," Bear said.

"This is why it is taking so long," Wrenchley replied.

Commander Aero's face appeared on the wall screen. "Telemetry says the popcorn tube is receiving too many dispense commands. It should receive one gentle pop signal. It is receiving pop-pop-pop-pop-pop."

Captain Bolts looked embarrassed. His screen-face went pink around the edges.

"I MAY HAVE PRESSED THE BIG BLUE BUTTON WHEN THE FIRST BOWL WAS TOO EMPTY," he said.

Papa floated closer to the control panel. He kept one boot hooked under a foot strap so he wouldn't drift.

"Buttons are good at doing exactly what we tell them," Papa said. "Even when we tell them too enthusiastically."

Bear studied the clear tube. The popcorn was not coming out in one steady stream. It came in bursts, always right after a little amber light blinked three times.

Blink-blink-blink. Thump-whirr-paff.

Blink-blink-blink. Thump-whirr-paff.

Bear pointed. "That light is telling it to go. But why three blinks?"

Papa smiled a tiny proud smile. "Good noticing."

Commander Aero checked another readout. "The tube's snack counter thinks there are three bowls waiting. But I only see one bowl attached."

Everyone looked at the bowl dock.

There was one bowl.

Then Beep bumped gently into the wall and rotated upside down.

Beep beeped twice and flashed two tiny bowl shapes on its screen-face.

Bear turned.

Two shiny bowl lids were stuck near the sensor, floating behind a loose Velcro flap. Their reflections made the sensor think extra bowls were waiting.

"Aha," said Papa.

"Aha," said Commander Aero.

"Aha?" said Captain Bolts hopefully.

Paff!

Popcorn bounced off all three of them.

Bear laughed so hard he had to hold the yellow rail.

"Aha," he said, "but we still have to catch the popcorn."

Chapter 3 — The Great Popcorn Plan

Papa did not say, "Hurry."

He said, "Pause."

So everybody paused.

Even Captain Bolts, who had one finger hovering near the big blue button again, pulled it back very slowly.

Papa breathed in through his nose. Bear breathed with him. In the busy white swirl of popcorn, it made a little quiet place right in the middle of the room.

"We have two problems," Papa said. "First, the sensor is seeing reflections and thinks there are extra bowls. Second, the popcorn already in the air needs somewhere safe to go."

Bear touched the rubber band in his pocket. Emergency bounce power.

His eyes brightened.

"Could we make a popcorn catcher? Not a grabber. A soft path. Like Little Red Bear's note. Figure out where it wants to go."

Wrenchley gasped. "A ceremonial snack trajectory!"

"Close," Papa said. "An airflow path."

Commander Aero brought up a station map. Thin blue lines showed the air moving from the fans, around the snack module, and back toward the filter wall.

"The popcorn is following the air currents," she said. "Mostly. Except when Wrenchley pokes it with the flag."

Wrenchley slowly lowered the flag.

Bear pointed to the map. "If we put a soft bag near that fan, the popcorn will drift into it. But not too close, because then it might block the air."

Papa nodded. "Exactly. We make a snack net that catches popcorn but lets air through."

Wrenchley produced a mesh laundry bag, four magnetic clips, and a ribbon that said BEST BOLTS, because Wrenchley believed every repair deserved a ribbon.

Bear and Papa clipped the mesh bag near the return wall. Captain Bolts steadied the corner with one round robot hand. Beep held the ribbon and tried very hard not to tie itself to the ceiling.

Beep made a very serious little beep. Its screen-face showed a ribbon and a thumbs-up symbol.

Then Papa turned one fan down a little, and Commander Aero adjusted another fan up by just one notch.

The popcorn changed course.

Not all at once.

First one piece drifted into the bag.

Then three.

Then a whole soft cloud of popcorn floated across the room and collected in the mesh with gentle little taps.

"It's working!" Bear said.

Captain Bolts's screen-face became a giant smile.

"SNACK WEATHER IS IMPROVING!"

Paff!

The tube fired again.

This time most of the popcorn went into the bag.

Most.

One piece bonked Captain Bolts right between his robot eyes.

"ACCEPTABLE," he said.

Now they had to stop the tube from firing. Bear floated to the sensor, moving hand over hand along the rail. He did not rush, even though popcorn tickled his ears and drifted through his hair.

The two shiny bowl lids were still behind the Velcro flap, flashing light into the bowl sensor.

Bear used the soft grabber. Slow. Gentle. Pinch, don't snatch.

He pulled one lid free.

The amber light blinked twice.

He pulled the second lid free.

The amber light blinked once.

Then Bear covered the shiny side of the lids with dull gray space tape, just like Papa had shown him in other station fixes.

The amber light stopped blinking.

The tube went quiet.

For one beautiful second, the whole RISS snack module was silent.

Then Beep made the smallest possible question-beep.

The tube made one final tiny sound.

Piff.

A single popcorn piece floated out and landed perfectly in Bear's open hand.

Bear looked at it.

Papa looked at it.

Captain Bolts looked at it.

"I think," Papa said, "the machine is saying thank you."

Chapter 4 — The Bowl That Counted Wrong

Fixing the flying popcorn was only half the job.

Papa always said a good repair did not stop at "it got quiet." A good repair asked, "Why did it get silly in the first place?"

So Bear and Papa stayed clipped to the snack module while Wrenchley vacuumed the last popcorn drifts with a soft tube that made a satisfied fwoomp sound.

Fwoomp.

"One kernel recovered," Wrenchley said.

Fwoomp.

"Two kernels recovered."

Fwoomp.

"This one resembles a tiny cloud with opinions."

Captain Bolts opened the popcorn control box. Inside were tidy wires, a small sensor lens, and a bowl counter with three little squares on its display.

Square one was green.

Square two was green.

Square three was green.

"But there is only one bowl," Bear said.

"The sensor got fooled by reflections," Papa said. "But it also believed the reflections too easily. Let's teach it to ask a better question."

Commander Aero leaned close on the screen. "Instead of 'Do I see something bowl-shaped?' it should ask, 'Is a real bowl locked into the dock?'"

Bear nodded. "Like when the RISS sensor had to learn what kind of thing it noticed."

"Exactly," Papa said.

Captain Bolts raised one finger. "I LIKE LEARNING. I DO NOT LIKE POPCORN IN MY ELBOW JOINTS."

"Those can both be true," Papa said.

Bear helped Papa test the bowl dock. A real bowl made a soft click when it locked in. A loose shiny lid made no click. A reflection made no click. Beep's head made a click, but only because Beep bumped into the dock by accident.

Beep gave an offended beep. Its screen-face displayed: NOT BOWL.

"Confirmed," said Commander Aero.

Papa changed the setting so the tube would only dispense when two things happened together: the sensor saw a bowl shape, and the dock felt the locking click.

Bear added one more idea.

"Could it wait after one bowl? Like count slowly before popping more?"

Papa's eyes warmed. "A cooldown timer. Very sensible."

Commander Aero typed the update through the tablet bridge. "Popcorn tube now requires real-bowl click plus five-second snack patience."

Captain Bolts looked nervous. "FIVE SECONDS IS A LONG TIME FOR SNACK PATIENCE."

Bear held up his hand and counted. "One snack-Mississippi. Two snack-Mississippi. Three snack-Mississippi. Four snack-Mississippi. Five snack-Mississippi."

The tube gave one polite thump.

A small, reasonable puff of popcorn fell into the bowl.

Not the ceiling.

Not Captain Bolts.

Not Beep.

The bowl.

Everyone cheered very quietly, because it was still a space station and loud cheering made tools drift out of pockets.

Wrenchley tied the BEST BOLTS ribbon around the popcorn tube.

"For bravery," Wrenchley said.

"For improved listening," Papa said.

"For not bonking me anymore," Captain Bolts added.

Bear took the first official fixed-tube popcorn piece. It was warm and salty and perfect.

"Science snack," he said.

Papa took one too. "The best kind."

Chapter 5 — Warm Lights Over Earth

When the repair was finished, the RISS looked cozy again.

The snack module no longer had popcorn orbiting the ceiling. The mesh bag was full of rescued popcorn. The bowl dock clicked properly. The big blue button had a new label that Commander Aero printed in calm blue letters:

PRESS ONCE. THEN COUNT TO FIVE.

Captain Bolts read it aloud three times.

"PRESS ONCE. THEN COUNT TO FIVE. PRESS ONCE. THEN COUNT TO FIVE. PRESS ONCE. THEN—"

"You've got it," Papa said gently.

Beep placed one single popcorn piece on its own head.

Beep made a fancy little beep and tilted its square head proudly.

Wrenchley gave Bear a sticker shaped like a popcorn kernel with a tiny wrench on it. The sticker said JUNIOR SNACK SYSTEM ENGINEER.

Papa got one too. His said SENIOR PATIENCE COUNTER.

Bear stuck his sticker carefully on his shirt. "I'm going to show Little Red Bear."

"He will be very impressed without moving at all," Papa said.

Commander Aero smiled from the wall screen. "Thank you, Bear. Thank you, Papa. The crew is safe, the snack system is stable, and the popcorn is behaving like popcorn again."

Captain Bolts floated closer. His screen-face was soft now, not worried.

"PLEASE TAKE SOME REPAIRED POPCORN FOR THE ROAD," he said.

Wrenchley handed over a sealed snack pouch labeled DEFINITELY NOT A SENSOR TARGET.

Papa clipped it to his belt. "Excellent labeling."

Bear looked out the round RISS window before they left. Earth curved below them, blue and white and quiet. The clouds looked soft enough to tuck in. The night side of the planet had tiny lights, like someone had sprinkled careful stars on the ground.

For a moment, nobody said anything.

Then Bear leaned against Papa's side.

"It got really silly," Bear said.

"It did," Papa said.

"But we didn't chase it."

"No. We watched where it was going."

Bear smiled sleepily. "And then we helped it go there better."

Papa put an arm around him. "That's a very good way to fix many things."

The tablet bridge opened with its soft silver hum. Bear waved to Captain Bolts, Wrenchley, Beep, and Commander Aero.

"Bye, RISS," Bear said. "Count to five."

"COUNTING IS NOW SNACK LAW," Captain Bolts promised.

Bear and Papa stepped through the light and back onto the deck at Papa Bear Home.

The evening air smelled like wood and leaves. Little Red Bear sat exactly where he had been, still and red and warm from the day.

Bear showed him the sticker.

Little Red Bear did not move. But the note at his base now had one extra line:

Well caught, Junior Snack System Engineer.

Bear grinned.

Inside, Papa poured the repaired popcorn into a small bowl. They ate just a little, because bedtime was close. Then Bear brushed his teeth, climbed into bed, and listened while Papa clipped the sticker to the shelf where morning could find it.

"Papa?" Bear whispered.

"Yes, Bear?"

"If the popcorn tube goes bonkers again, Captain Bolts should count snack-Mississippi."

Papa tucked the blanket under Bear's chin. "I think that's exactly right."

Bear's eyes closed.

The house was quiet. The deck was quiet. Far above, the RISS was quiet too, with its popcorn tucked safely in bowls and its crew counting slowly whenever snacks were near.

And Bear slept, warm and loved, under the soft lights of home.

*The End.*
