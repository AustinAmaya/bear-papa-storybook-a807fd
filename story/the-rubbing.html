2026-06-11

The Rubbing

---

---

Preamble

Bear had been thinking about the Observation Stone for three days.

He sat on the deck with his blue notebook open to the page where he'd drawn the circle — the carved circle etched into the stone's flat face. He'd drawn it as best he could from memory, but something about it nagged at him.

"I didn't get it right," he said to himself.

Little Red Bear stood on the railing nearby. "What didn't you get right?"

"The lines inside the circle," Bear said. "There were lines. Thin ones. I couldn't tell if they were cracks or carvings."

"Could be both," Little Red Bear said. "Things can be two things at once."

Bear closed the notebook. That was a very good point. He needed to go back and look properly.

---

Chapter 1

Cheetah arrived the way she always did.

THWUMP.

Bear was halfway across the yard when she landed in front of him — a full flying pounce from the fence post, spots gleaming in the morning light.

"You called," Cheetah announced, as though she had been waiting for exactly this moment. "Where are we going?"

"The Observation Stone," Bear said. "I need to make a rubbing."

Cheetah tilted her head. "A rubbing?"

"You put paper flat on something carved," Bear explained, "and rub the side of a pencil over it. Whatever's carved shows up on the paper in the pencil marks."

Cheetah's eyes went wide. "Like a copy?"

"Like a true copy," Bear said. "Better than drawing from memory."

Papa came through the back door with his thermos and his flashlight clipped to his belt. He was already wearing his woods shoes. "Ready?" he said.

"You knew?" Bear asked.

Papa smiled. "Little Red Bear mentioned you'd been staring at that notebook page all morning."

---

Chapter 2

They went through the leaning-pine shortcut — the army-crawl gap where you had to get low and push through the brush. Cheetah went through first. She was faster at it now. A few weeks ago she'd knocked her chin. Today she slipped through like water.

"I've been practicing," she said on the other side, standing up and flicking a leaf off her ear.

Bear grinned. He knew she had.

The Whispering Woods were quiet this morning. The light came down through the canopy in long pale shafts, landing on the path in gold coins that shifted as the branches moved. Bear counted the path markers in his notebook as they walked — two bent ferns, the root gap near the Great Old Tree, the place where the ground got darker and the air smelled like wet stone.

The Great Old Tree rose up on their left, massive and ancient, bark grooved so deep you could fit four fingers in the ridges. Bear touched it as he passed, just like always. Just to say hello.

"Still there," Cheetah said.

"Always there," Bear said.

They found the Observation Stone a few minutes later, right where it had always been — a flat slab of gray rock in a natural clearing where the canopy opened to a long oval of sky.

---

Chapter 3

The carved marks were better than Bear remembered.

He crouched down with his nose close to the stone's surface. The circle was about as wide as his open hand. Inside it, thin lines radiated outward from the center like spokes — but not evenly spaced. Some were closer together. And around the outside of the circle, there were smaller marks. Dots, maybe. Or short scratches.

"Cheetah," Bear said. "Come look."

Cheetah padded over carefully and looked where Bear pointed. "Ohhh," she said softly. "That's not just a circle."

"No," Bear said. "It's something."

Papa crouched next to them. He studied the marks for a long moment without speaking. That was how Papa thought — quietly, from the outside in.

"The lines don't go all the way to the edge," Papa said finally. "They stop short. And those outer marks — see how they cluster on one side?"

Bear nodded. He was already tearing a careful page from the back of his notebook. Not the map pages. The blank pages at the end, the ones he kept for exactly this kind of thing.

"Hold the paper flat," Bear told Cheetah. "Don't let it move."

Cheetah pressed both hands to the paper's edges with great concentration. She did not breathe.

Bear laid the pencil on its side and began to rub in slow, even strokes.

---

Chapter 4

The rubbing came up like magic.

Every line that Bear had barely been able to see before appeared clearly now in gray pencil — the circle, the spokes, the outer marks. Cheetah gasped. Even Papa leaned forward.

Bear held the rubbing up to the light. Steady hands. He turned it slowly.

Then he put it beside his notebook, open to the page with the old map — the one he'd carefully copied from the oilskin map they'd found in the Great Old Tree hollow. He laid the two side by side in the grass.

Cheetah looked from one to the other. Then she sat back on her heels. "Bear."

"I see it," Bear said.

"What?" Papa said, though from his voice Bear could tell Papa already saw it too.

"The marks on the stone," Bear said. "These ones here —" he pointed to the outer cluster on the rubbing "— they're the same as these marks on the old map. Here. In the corner."

Papa brought his face very close to the notebook page. The corner of the old map showed a small cluster of the same short marks and dots. They had always thought it was a decorative border. Or maybe damage from age.

"Not damage," Papa said quietly.

"Not decoration," Bear said.

"The same hand made both of these," Papa said. "Whoever carved this stone also drew this map."

The woods were very quiet.

"The friend from a long time ago," Cheetah said. She said it the way you say something when you've been waiting to understand it for a while.

"Yes," Bear said. He wrote carefully in his notebook: *The Observation Stone circle symbols match the border marks on the old map. Same maker.*

---

Chapter 5

They stayed by the Observation Stone for a while.

Cheetah lay on her side in the clearing, watching the oval of sky above them. Papa drank from his thermos. Bear kept the rubbing flat between two notebook pages so it wouldn't crease.

"Do you think there are more?" Cheetah asked.

"More stones with carvings?" Bear said.

"More things that match," Cheetah said. "Things we haven't connected yet."

Bear thought about the old map. Six paths. Three still unwalked. The Listening Stone's hum. The Shepherd Star. The 1923 note in Papa's study. The handwriting that matched.

"Yes," Bear said. "I think there are."

Papa looked up at the canopy gap — the oval of sky, bright blue this morning. The same gap the Shepherd Star passed through on clear nights. He didn't say anything. He just looked at it with that expression he got when he was holding a thought carefully, the way you hold something fragile.

"Papa," Bear said. "What are you thinking?"

"I'm thinking," Papa said, "that whoever this friend was — they were very careful. They didn't just leave one clue. They left a system. A whole system, spread across the woods and the sky." He looked at Bear. "And we're only just starting to read it."

Bear tucked the rubbing safely into his notebook.

Cheetah stood, stretched all the way from nose to tail-tip, and then walked over and bumped her head gently against Bear's shoulder.

"Good adventure," she said.

"Good adventure," Bear agreed.

They walked back through the Whispering Woods together, past the Great Old Tree (Bear touched it), through the Pebble Ground (Cheetah's clearing, where the flat stones caught the afternoon light just so), and out through the leaning-pine shortcut into the yard.

Little Red Bear was still on the railing.

"Did you figure it out?" Little Red Bear called.

"Part of it," Bear called back.

"That's the best kind of figuring," Little Red Bear said.

Bear smiled. He thought that was probably true.

*The End.*
