Bear Teaches Cheetah the Telescope

---

---

Preamble

Bear's telescope was black and white and lived on a shelf in his room next to his extra pencils and the smooth stone he'd found at Quiet Creek.

He'd had it for two years. He knew how to find the moon, Jupiter, and three of Jupiter's moons on a good night. He knew how to find the Shepherd Star — Sigma Pictoris, class K dwarf — even when there was thin cloud cover, because he'd practiced.

He had never let anyone else use it.

Not because he was selfish. He just hadn't thought of it.

Until Cheetah had asked.

She'd asked at the end of the fog adventure, when they were walking back through the yard. "I've never looked through a telescope," she'd said, and then she'd said it so carefully and quietly — like she wasn't sure if it was allowed — that Bear had looked at her and realized he'd been keeping something wonderful to himself without meaning to.

"Next clear night," he'd said.

Tonight was clear.

---

Chapter 1

Cheetah arrived at dusk, when the sky was the color of a bruised peach.

THWUMP. But softer than usual. She landed on the deck railing instead of on Bear. Bear appreciated this. The telescope was delicate.

"Is it ready?" she asked.

"Almost," Bear said. He was carefully tightening the focuser knob — not too tight, just snug. Papa stood nearby with his thermos, watching Bear work with the quiet expression that meant *he's doing it right, don't help.*

The telescope was mounted on its tripod, pointed northeast — above the tallest pine. Bear knew that direction by heart now. He'd found the Shepherd Star from the deck a dozen times.

"Does it go in your eye?" Cheetah asked, eyeing the eyepiece.

"Yes," Bear said. "One eye closed. The other one open, relaxed. Not squinting. Just... looking."

"What do you see first?"

"Nothing," Bear said, "until your eye adjusts. Then the star pops in."

Cheetah looked uncertain. "What if my eye doesn't adjust?"

"It will," Bear said. "Everyone's does. You just have to wait."

Papa said nothing. He drank from his thermos and watched the sky get darker.

---

Chapter 2

Bear found it first.

He moved the telescope slowly, tracking the familiar pattern: find the bright reference star to the northwest, drift east, pick up the K-dwarf's slightly warm color. The Shepherd Star appeared in the eyepiece — a steady point of amber-gold light, warmer than the white stars around it.

"Got it," Bear said. He stepped back from the eyepiece but kept his hand on the tripod so it wouldn't drift. "Come here. Don't touch the tube — just put your eye up to the black circle."

Cheetah crept forward with enormous care. She lowered herself until her eye was at the eyepiece level. She looked.

She didn't say anything for a long moment.

"I don't see—" she started.

"Wait," Bear said.

Silence.

"Oh," Cheetah said.

Then, softer: "Oh."

Bear smiled. He knew exactly what that was. That was the moment the star popped in.

"It's golden," Cheetah said. "I thought stars were white. This one is — it's like a tiny candle. Way far away but warm."

"That's because it's a K dwarf," Bear said. "Cooler than our sun. So it looks orange-gold instead of white-blue."

"Bear," Cheetah said. She hadn't moved from the eyepiece. "This is wonderful."

---

Chapter 3

Papa pulled a chair over and sat down. This was his lecture-chair position. Bear liked it. Papa only sat like that when he was about to explain something properly.

"That star," Papa said, "is about sixty light-years away. You're looking at light that left the star sixty years ago. The light you're seeing right now started traveling before I was born."

Cheetah pulled back from the eyepiece and turned to look at Papa. She sat down on the railing. Bear sat on the deck rail post beside her.

"What we know," Papa continued, touching the rim of his thermos with one finger, counting off, "is this: a slow-moving cloud of material sometimes passes in front of it. It's bluish-white. It doesn't behave the way random clouds of space dust behave. It moves. Consistently. On a schedule."

"How do we know that?" Cheetah asked.

"A note in a chart from 1923," Bear said. He could recite it. "'4 hr anomalous brightening. 1923. Cause unknown.' The handwriting on that note matches the handwriting on the old map from the Great Old Tree hollow."

Cheetah stared at him. "The same person."

"The same person," Bear said. "The friend from a long time ago."

"They were watching the same star we're watching," Cheetah said. It came out very quiet, the way big things come out.

"From before 1923," Papa said. "Maybe long before. And they left us the Observation Stone — right under the canopy gap where the star is visible in the Whispering Woods. They left us the map. They left us the note."

"They left us everything," Cheetah said. She was quiet for a moment. Then: "Why?"

No one answered. The deck was quiet. The Shepherd Star glowed in the eyepiece, steady and amber, sixty years of old light.

---

Chapter 4

Bear had his notebook out before Papa finished speaking.

He was writing fast, in his small careful handwriting — the kind he used for important things. Cheetah watched him write.

"Read it to me?" she asked.

Bear read: "'What we know about the Shepherd Star. 1. Class K dwarf, Sigma Pictoris. Sixty light-years. Amber-gold color. 2. Periodic bluish-white cloud — not random, too consistent. Moves toward the Whispering Woods when it appears. 3. Cloud confirmed in 1923, confirmed by us. Same note, same person as the old map. 4. Observation Stone marks the viewing spot in the woods. The friend from a long time ago built it. 5. The cloud and the Listening Stone — possibly connected. Sky and ground, same source.'"

Cheetah's ears had gone very still, the way they did when she was thinking hard.

"Sky and ground," she said. "The cloud up there and the hum down there."

"That's the theory," Bear said. "Papa and I thought of it when we were at the Listening Stone. The hum is underground water — we know that. But what if the same... force, or entity, or — something — is connected to both? What if the cloud appears when something changes underground?"

"Or what if the underground and the sky are both responses to the same source?" Papa said quietly.

"Something else," Cheetah said. She looked up at the Shepherd Star — visible now without the telescope, just a faint amber-tinted point in the dark. "Something none of you has named yet."

Bear looked at her. Then he wrote in his notebook: *Cheetah said: 'Something none of you has named yet.'*

"That's going in?" Cheetah said.

"That's going in," Bear said.

---

Chapter 5

They took turns until the night got cold.

Papa looked first — he adjusted the focus slightly, something he'd always been careful not to do when Bear was using it, so Bear appreciated that Papa asked before touching the knob. Then Cheetah looked again. Then Bear.

From the deck, the Shepherd Star was visible but you couldn't see the canopy gap that framed it in the Whispering Woods. Here it was just a star in open sky. But Bear could picture the Observation Stone beneath it, and the hum of the Listening Stone somewhere to the northwest, and the Great Old Tree with its deep grooves and the hollow where the old map had waited for them.

And the friend from a long time ago — watching this same star, from the ground of the woods, writing a note in 1923 that no one read for a hundred years.

Until Bear did.

"Thank you," Cheetah said. She wasn't talking to any of them in particular. She was talking to the star, or maybe to the deck, or maybe to the whole strange chain of things that had led them here.

"Thank you for what?" Bear asked.

"For letting me look," Cheetah said. She glanced at Bear. "Both of you."

Bear updated his notebook one last time. He drew a small diagram: the telescope eyepiece and the amber star beyond it, and below that, a small cross-section of the Observation Stone circle from the rubbing. Above the star he wrote: *light 60 years old.* Below the stone he wrote: *carved some time before 1923.* And between them, a single word in the middle of the page:

*Connected?*

He showed Cheetah.

"Definitely connected," she said.

Bear smiled and closed the notebook. The Shepherd Star glowed quietly overhead, steady as it had always been, warm and gold and patient with all of them.

*The End.*
