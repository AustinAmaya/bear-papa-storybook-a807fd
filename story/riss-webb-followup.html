The RISS Follow-Up Mystery

Preamble — The Tablet Door

The morning after the Wolf Gap adventure, Bear was still thinking about the stars.

He had his blue notebook open on the kitchen table. At the top of one page he had written, very carefully:

Wolf Gap Crescent = dust smile around baby planet?

Under that he had drawn the blink pattern.

Blink. Blink. Pause.

Blink. Blink. Pause.

Papa came in with two bowls of cereal and one very serious spoon tucked behind his ear.

Bear pointed at it. “Papa.”

Papa looked left. Looked right. “What?”

“You have a spoon on your head.”

“Ah,” Papa said. “That explains why my ear felt breakfasty.”

Bear giggled so hard his pencil rolled off the table.

Then the science tablet chimed.

Not a normal chime.

A deep, bright Machine World chime.

The screen filled with silver light, and Commander Aero appeared. His polished metal frame gleamed like a moonlit robot statue. Behind him, a star map spun over the deck of The Horizon.

“Honorary Commander Bear,” Commander Aero said. “Papa. I apologize for interrupting breakfast.”

Papa rescued the spoon from his ear. “Breakfast can be rescheduled for science.”

Bear sat up straight. “Is it the Wolf Gap Crescent?”

“Yes,” Commander Aero said. “And no.”

Bear made the face he made when a puzzle had elbows.

Commander Aero tapped the star map. A tiny red dot blinked on one side. Then a little blue-white line flickered near Earth.

“After Webb observed the Wolf Gap Crescent, the Robot International Space Station reported a follow-up mystery. Their sensors are seeing a similar blink pattern reflected through Earth orbit.”

Papa leaned closer. “Reflected? Not coming from the same star?”

“Correct,” Commander Aero said. “The RISS robots need help. Their instruments are safe, their crew is safe, but the data makes no sense yet.”

Bear whispered the best sentence in science.

“Yet.”

Commander Aero held up one shining hand. The tablet screen widened, becoming deep and bright like a doorway made of pixels.

“My tablet bridge can transfer you directly to the Robot International Space Station. All science. No magic. It is a temporary matter-to-data transport field with very strict safety rails.”

Papa looked at Bear. “That means we do not jump until we choose our plan.”

Bear bounced once in his chair. “We choose fast, though, right?”

Papa smiled. “Scientifically fast.”

Commander Aero’s voice grew warm and official. “How should we enter the RISS investigation?”

What should Bear and Papa do first? Option one: Bring Bear’s Wolf Gap notebook and compare the new blink pattern to the old one. Option two: Bring the mini telescope filter kit and test whether the mystery signal changes color. Option three: Ask Commander Aero to send them straight to the RISS sensor deck, where the robots are already arguing with the data.

Chapter 1 — The Arguing Data

Bear ran to his room so fast his cereal spoon nearly spun in its bowl.

“Telescope!” he called.

“Filter kit!” Papa called back.

“Notebook!”

“Already on the table!”

“Snack?” Bear shouted from the hallway.

Papa paused. “For us or for science?”

“Both!”

Papa put two granola bars into his pocket, because he was a practical scientist and also a practical papa.

Bear came back carrying his black-and-white telescope case with both hands. Papa had the little filter kit: red, blue, green, yellow, and smoky gray squares in a padded box. Commander Aero waited inside the tablet doorway, patient as a clock.

“Ready?” Commander Aero asked.

Bear looked at Papa.

Papa knelt down, because tablet jumping deserved eye-level talking.

“We will feel a little fizz, then a little float,” Papa said. “If anything feels weird, you say so. If we arrive in microgravity, tiny pushes only.”

Bear nodded. “Tiny pushes. Big pushes make big zooms.”

“Exactly.”

Commander Aero lifted one polished hand. “Tablet bridge opening.”

The kitchen stretched into silver pixels.

Bear held the telescope case tight.

Papa held Bear’s shoulder gently.

Then they stepped forward.

Fizzzzip!

For one second, Bear felt like a math problem being carefully moved from one page to another.

Then his shoes left the floor.

“Oh!” Bear said.

He and Papa drifted through a round hatch into the Robot International Space Station.

The RISS was bright and busy. Silver handrails lined the walls. Velcro patches held little tool bags in place. Color-coded pipes ran along the ceiling, including one marked with a carrot sticker.

A robot with a captain’s badge spun gently upside down near a console.

“Visitors aboard!” the robot said. “Captain Bolts welcomes— no, wait— rotates— no, hold please.”

He grabbed a rail and turned himself right-side up.

“Captain Bolts welcomes you aboard.”

A smaller robot with three tool arms zipped past, muttering, “Data cannot argue. Data can only disagree loudly through charts.”

“That’s Wrenchley,” Captain Bolts said.

“I heard that,” Wrenchley said.

Another little robot hovered by a screen saying, “Beep. Beep-beep. Pattern conflict. Beep.”

“That’s Beep,” Captain Bolts said. “He is worried in binary.”

Papa caught a blue handhold with two fingers, slowing himself. Bear copied him with a tiny careful push and grabbed the next rail.

“Nice,” Papa said.

Bear grinned. “Tiny fast is still fast.”

Commander Aero appeared on a wall screen. “RISS crew, Honorary Commander Bear and Papa have arrived with optical filters.”

Wrenchley threw all three arms upward. This made him spin backward.

“Excellent! Finally! Someone who understands that the blue graph is clearly wrong!”

Captain Bolts beeped his chest badge. “The blue graph is not wrong. The red graph is dramatic.”

Beep said, “Beep-beep-beep,” which sounded a lot like, “Everybody please stop.”

Bear looked at Papa.

Papa took a slow breath in through his nose and out through his mouth.

Bear copied him.

Then Bear said, “Maybe the graphs are not fighting. Maybe they’re talking about different pieces.”

The robots went quiet.

Even Wrenchley stopped spinning.

Papa smiled. “That is a very good beginning.”

Captain Bolts guided them to the sensor deck. A wide window showed Earth glowing blue and white below. Beyond it, space was black and full of tiny steady stars.

On the main screen, three lines blinked.

Red: blink. blink. pause.

Blue: blink-pause-blink.

Infrared: warm… warm… quiet.

Bear frowned. “That’s like Wolf Gap, but not the same.”

“Correct,” Commander Aero said from the wall screen. “The timing is shifted.”

Papa opened Bear’s filter kit and clipped the blue filter over a small station viewing scope. Bear set his notebook beside the console with a Velcro strip.

“Let’s slow it down,” Papa said. “What are we actually measuring?”

Wrenchley pointed at the screen. “Reflected sunlight from something passing below the station.”

Captain Bolts pointed at another screen. “And a faint infrared echo from something much farther away.”

Beep flashed. “Beep. Similar to Wolf Gap Crescent. Not same. Beep.”

Bear pressed his hands together so he would not float off from excitement.

“So there are two mysteries stacked on top of each other?”

Papa’s eyes brightened.

“Maybe,” he said. “A near thing and a far thing. The trick is separating them.”

Bear looked at the filters.

Then he looked out at Earth.

Then at the screen where the data still blinked in three not-quite-matching rhythms.

“This is going to be fun,” Bear said.

Wrenchley whispered, “The data is still arguing.”

Bear shook his head. “Nope. We just haven’t asked it the right question yet.”

What should Bear do next? Option one: Use the color filters on the RISS viewing scope to separate the nearby reflected light from the far infrared signal. Option two: Ask Beep to replay the sensor data slowly, one blink at a time, so everyone can compare rhythms calmly. Option three: Float with Wrenchley to the outside observation rail and inspect whether something near the station is reflecting sunlight.

Chapter 2 — The Shiny Thing Outside

Captain Bolts’ badge blinked yellow.

“Exterior inspection requires safety checklist,” he said. “Checklist requires calm voices. Calm voices require nobody shouting at the blue graph.”

Wrenchley folded all three tool arms against his round metal body.

“I will not shout at the blue graph while outside,” he said.

Papa raised one eyebrow.

Wrenchley added, “I will also not whisper insults at it.”

“Good enough to begin,” Papa said.

Bear tucked his notebook under a Velcro strap on the sensor deck and clipped the smoky gray filter case to his belt loop. Papa checked the little tether on Bear’s suit harness. The RISS had visitor suits folded in a wall cabinet, soft and white with blue handgrips on the wrists. Bear’s helmet had a tiny sticker on the inside that said: BREATHE SLOW. LOOK TWICE.

Papa tapped the sticker. “Good advice.”

Bear nodded. “Breathe slow. Look twice. Don’t zoom into space.”

“Especially that last part,” Papa said.

Cheetah was not there this time to carry them space-fast, but the station robots had an exterior crawl rail. It ran along the outside of the RISS like a metal sidewalk for careful hands.

Wrenchley clipped his tether to the airlock ring. Then he clipped Bear’s tether. Then Papa checked both clips. Then Captain Bolts checked Papa checking both clips. Then Beep checked everyone by making three satisfied little sounds.

“Beep. Beep. Beep.”

Commander Aero’s voice came through the helmet speakers. “I will monitor from the bridge link. Bear, remember: move by pulling on rails, not pushing into open space.”

Bear gave a thumbs-up. “Pull, don’t launch.”

The airlock hummed. The inner door closed. The outer door opened.

And there was Earth.

Bear forgot to talk for a second.

Blue oceans curved below them. White clouds swirled like cream in a cup. The black of space wrapped around everything, huge and quiet and bright with stars.

Wrenchley whispered, “I have seen this six thousand four hundred twelve times.”

Bear looked at him.

Wrenchley’s little camera eyes glowed softer. “It is still my favorite window.”

Bear smiled inside his helmet.

Together they moved along the exterior rail. Wrenchley went first, magnet-clink, magnet-clink. Bear followed by pulling hand over hand, slow and careful. Papa stayed just inside the airlock window, watching with one hand resting on the glass. He was close enough to see. Close enough to help. Far enough to let Bear do the job.

“Sensor direction?” Bear asked.

Wrenchley spun one tool arm into a pointer. “Down-station, sunward side. We see reflection spikes every ninety-two minutes, matching station orbit. That means something close to Earth or near the station, not faraway space.”

Bear thought. “Because if it happens every time we go around Earth—”

“—then it is connected to our orbit,” Wrenchley finished.

“That’s not arguing data,” Bear said. “That’s data giving us a hint.”

Wrenchley was quiet for one whole magnet-clink.

“I may owe the blue graph a small apology.”

They reached the observation rail. A small viewing scope pointed outward, mounted on a bracket. Beside it was a flat sensor panel with red, green, and blue indicators.

Bear clipped the smoky gray filter over the scope, just like Papa had shown him.

“Filter attached,” Bear said.

Wrenchley adjusted the scope angle. “Sunlight reflection spike expected in ten seconds.”

Bear looked through.

At first, he saw only stars and the curved blue edge of Earth.

Then—flash.

A tiny bright speck swept past the field of view.

“There!” Bear said. “Small thing. Shiny. Moving fast.”

“Space debris?” Wrenchley asked.

“Maybe,” Bear said. “But it was too regular. Can we see it again?”

“Next pass in approximately forty-six seconds due to station rotation and object angle.”

Bear grinned. “That means yes, but robot fancy.”

Wrenchley made a sound that might have been a laugh if wrenches could laugh.

They waited. Bear kept his breathing slow. His hands stayed wrapped around the rail.

Flash.

This time Bear saw shape.

Not a rock.

Not a lost bolt.

A flat silver square, tumbling slowly.

“It’s like foil,” Bear said. “Or a panel.”

Wrenchley’s tool arms froze.

“Panel,” he said. “Panel is a concerning word.”

Commander Aero’s voice came in. “Can you identify the object?”

Bear squinted through the filter. “It has stripes. Gold on one side. Silver on the other.”

Captain Bolts crackled over the speaker from inside. “RISS inventory search: a small thermal blanket patch was logged missing after last week’s maintenance drill.”

Wrenchley made a very tiny beep.

Papa’s voice was calm in Bear’s helmet. “Thermal blanket material is reflective. It could make a bright visible flash when sunlight hits it.”

Bear looked back toward the sensor deck window. “So that’s the near signal.”

“Likely,” Papa said. “But not the far infrared echo.”

Wrenchley pointed one tool arm toward the distant stars.

“The far signal remains after subtracting the blanket flashes.”

Bear felt the puzzle click into two pieces.

“The shiny blanket is making the station blink,” he said. “But something far away is still warming.”

Commander Aero answered, “Correct. You have isolated the first signal.”

Wrenchley made a delighted whirr. “Data is no longer arguing. Data is taking turns!”

The silver square flashed again, tumbling like a tiny lost kite in orbit.

Bear looked at it carefully.

“Can we catch it?” he asked.

Wrenchley extended a soft capture loop from one arm. “We can try if Captain Bolts approves a slow rail maneuver.”

Captain Bolts answered immediately. “Approved if slow means slow.”

Papa added, “And if Bear feels steady.”

Bear checked himself. His breathing was calm. His tether was clipped. His hands were steady.

“I feel steady,” he said.

The bright patch drifted closer on its next pass. Wrenchley anchored both magnetic feet. Bear held the rail with one hand and used the other to help guide the soft loop.

“Three meters,” Wrenchley said.

“Two,” said Bear.

“One.”

The loop opened.

The blanket patch floated in.

Wrenchley closed the loop as gently as catching a bubble.

“Captured!” Bear shouted.

Then he remembered calm voices.

“Captured,” he said again, quieter but still extremely happy.

Inside the station, Beep cheered in beeps. Captain Bolts spun once and pretended it was on purpose.

But on the main sensor screen, visible through the window, the infrared line still pulsed.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

Bear watched it through the glass.

“The near thing is solved,” he said. “Now we need Webb again.”

Papa smiled from inside the airlock.

“Looks that way, Chief Telescope Officer.”

Commander Aero’s voice became thoughtful. “The far infrared echo is similar to the Wolf Gap Crescent, but colder and slower. We need to know whether it is dust, ice, or something else reflecting and warming near another young system.”

Wrenchley held up the captured thermal blanket patch.

“One mystery in the loop,” he said. “One mystery still in the stars.”

Bear looked from the tiny patch to the huge dark sky.

“Then let’s go ask the stars better questions.”

What should Bear and Papa do next? Option one: Bring the captured thermal blanket patch inside and use it to teach the robots how to subtract the near reflection from the data. Option two: Ask Commander Aero to contact Webb right away for a new infrared spectrum of the far signal. Option three: Have Bear compare the far signal’s warm-warm-quiet rhythm with the Wolf Gap Crescent pattern in his notebook before they request Webb time.

Chapter 3 — Warm-Warm-Quiet

Back inside the airlock, the first sound Bear heard was Beep celebrating.

“Beep-beep! Beep-beep-beep!”

Captain Bolts translated. “He says, ‘The shiny problem is contained and nobody became a tiny moon.’”

“That is exactly what I hoped he said,” Papa answered.

Wrenchley floated through the hatch holding the captured thermal blanket patch inside a soft clear pouch. The patch was no bigger than a placemat, silver on one side and gold on the other, with crinkly layers like a very serious space sandwich wrapper.

Bear unclipped his helmet. His fur was a little flattened around his ears.

Papa smiled. “Exterior scientist hair.”

Bear patted his head. “Worth it.”

Commander Aero appeared on the wall screen again, her silver wings tucked neatly behind her shoulders. The name label under her picture blinked: COMMANDER AERO.

Bear pointed at it. “Aero like aerodynamic.”

“Correct,” Commander Aero said warmly. “A useful word for things that move through air. Less useful in vacuum, but still my name.”

Wrenchley looked at the label, then at Bear. “I have updated my spelling subroutine.”

“Good,” Bear said. “Names matter.”

Papa squeezed Bear’s shoulder once. Then he nodded toward the sensor deck. “Now. Option three?”

Bear opened his blue notebook to the Wolf Gap page. The old pattern was already there.

Blink. Blink. Pause.

Below it, he wrote the RISS far signal.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

Then he drew two boxes.

Wolf Gap: bright, bright, pause. Warm after. RISS far signal: warm, warm, quiet. No bright flash after blanket removed.

Captain Bolts hovered respectfully upside down. Wrenchley tried to hover respectfully right-side up, but kept drifting sideways. Beep dimmed his lights so the notebook page was easier to see.

Papa said, “Let’s compare what is the same first.”

Bear tapped the page. “Two beats, then a rest.”

“Good.”

“And both need infrared to understand them.”

“Very good.”

Commander Aero added, “Both appear connected to material around young star systems.”

Bear wrote: young systems?

Papa said, “Now differences.”

Bear looked at the main screen. The near reflection spikes were gone now that the thermal blanket patch had been captured. The red and blue visible-light lines had calmed down. But the infrared line still pulsed with its soft rhythm.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

“This one is colder,” Bear said.

Wrenchley zipped to a console and pulled up a temperature chart. “Correct! Colder than the Wolf Gap Crescent dust by a lot of degrees. I will say the exact number if someone wants me to say seventeen digits.”

“No thank you,” Captain Bolts said quickly.

Bear grinned. “Maybe later.”

Papa pointed at the chart. “Colder usually means farther from the star, or made of different material, or both.”

Bear wrote: farther out? different stuff?

Then he paused.

“Could it be ice?”

The room went quiet in the good way. The science way.

Papa’s eyebrows rose. “That is a strong hypothesis.”

Commander Aero brought up a Machine World model: a small red star, a dusty inner ring, and a wide outer belt that glowed faintly in infrared.

“An icy belt around a young star could warm unevenly,” Aero said. “If clumps of ice and dust rotate into view, Webb might see a warm-warm-quiet pattern.”

Captain Bolts pressed his badge. “RISS question: why would it repeat like a pattern instead of just glowing?”

Bear held up two fingers. “Because maybe there are two big clumps.”

Papa nodded. “Two brighter regions in the belt. As the system rotates, one clump faces us, then another, then the dim gap.”

Wrenchley spun in place. “Warm. Warm. Quiet! Clump. Clump. Gap!”

Beep flashed green. “Beep!”

Bear wrote that down too.

Clump. Clump. Gap.

Then he looked at the Webb request form on the station screen. It had many boxes. Target coordinates. Observation window. Instrument mode. Safety angle. Estimated time.

Bear swallowed.

“That is a lot of boxes.”

Papa leaned beside him. “Big tools need careful asking.”

“Like asking to borrow someone’s best microscope?”

“Exactly. Except the microscope is in space, very cold, and has a sunshield the size of a tennis court.”

Bear nodded slowly. “So we tell them what we already checked.”

Commander Aero’s wings made a soft adjustment. “Yes. We do not ask Webb to solve the whole messy puzzle. We ask Webb to test a clear hypothesis.”

Bear sat up straighter. “Hypothesis: the far signal is an icy belt with two warmer clumps and a gap around a young star system.”

Papa smiled. “Beautifully said.”

Wrenchley raised one tool arm. “Should we mention that I caught the blanket?”

Captain Bolts said, “Only in the section called possible contamination removed.”

Wrenchley sighed happily. “That is my favorite section.”

Together, they filled in the request. Papa handled the coordinates. Commander Aero added Machine World sensor timing. Captain Bolts attached the RISS data after the near-reflection subtraction. Beep checked the rhythm. Wrenchley added one neat photograph of the captured thermal blanket patch, labeled: NOT THE STAR THING.

Bear wrote the summary sentence.

Far infrared signal remains after nearby reflection removed. Pattern suggests two cold clumps and one gap in an outer icy belt. Webb spectrum needed to test for water ice and dust.

Papa read it twice.

Then he looked at Bear. “Ready to ask?”

Bear put one finger over the send button, but did not press yet.

“What if Webb says no?” he asked.

Papa’s voice stayed gentle. “Then we wait, improve the request, or use other tools first. No is not the end of science. It is sometimes part of scheduling.”

Commander Aero nodded. “And sometimes a good request becomes a yes later.”

Bear breathed in. Breathed out.

He pressed send.

The request shot from the RISS to Earth, then into the network that talked to one of the greatest telescopes ever built.

For a moment, nobody spoke.

Then the screen changed.

Request received. Reviewing target visibility.

Wrenchley whispered, “The boxes liked us.”

Captain Bolts whispered back, “The boxes are processing us.”

Beep whispered, “Beep.”

Bear looked at the warm-warm-quiet line still pulsing on the screen.

“We asked a better question,” he said.

Papa smiled. “Yes, we did.”

What should Bear and Papa do while Webb’s team reviews the request? Option one: Build a small physical model of the icy belt with beads, Velcro, and a flashlight so the robots can see the clump-clump-gap idea. Option two: Help Beep clean up the sensor display so the far signal is shown clearly without the removed blanket reflection. Option three: Visit the RISS cupola window with Captain Bolts and look for the target star field while they wait.

Chapter 4 — The Clump-Clump-Gap Machine

Wrenchley heard the words “physical model” and became immediately taller.

Not actually taller. He was still the same round robot with three tool arms. But all his lights brightened, which made him seem taller in spirit.

“Model supplies!” he announced. “I have model supplies. I have excellent model supplies. I have supplies that have waited their whole lives to become a model.”

Captain Bolts folded his arms. “Please do not use anything labeled life support.”

Wrenchley froze.

“One time,” he said.

Papa pointed gently. “Let’s start with safe things.”

Beep opened a storage drawer with a happy chirp. Inside were Velcro dots, colored beads, string loops, a small flashlight, two foam rings, three magnetic clips, and a packet labeled EDUCATIONAL ORBIT SNACKS.

Bear picked up the packet. “Are these snacks or science?”

Captain Bolts leaned close. “Both, but mostly neither. They are extremely chewy.”

Bear set them down carefully.

Papa took one foam ring and placed it on the table. In microgravity, placing something on a table did not mean much, so Beep stuck Velcro dots underneath it. The ring stayed put.

“This is the icy belt,” Papa said.

Bear chose pale blue beads for ice and gray beads for dust. He stuck them around the foam ring in a gentle scatter. Then he made two thicker bunches on one side and one empty gap on the other.

“Clump,” Bear said, pressing the first bunch down.

“Clump,” he said, pressing the second bunch.

He left the quiet space bare.

“Gap.”

Wrenchley whispered, “It is beautiful and statistically uneven.”

Commander Aero watched from the wall screen. “That unevenness is the point. If the belt were smooth, the infrared glow would stay steadier. Clumps make changes.”

Captain Bolts dimmed the station lights. Papa clicked on the small flashlight and aimed it at the model star in the middle: a yellow bead stuck to the table with an impressive amount of Velcro.

Bear slowly turned the ring around the star.

“When the first clump faces us,” he said, “warm.”

Beep made the graph line rise.

“When the second clump faces us,” Bear said, “warm again.”

Beep made the graph rise again.

“When the gap faces us…”

The graph dipped.

“Quiet,” Bear said.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

Wrenchley spun in a tiny circle. “The model agrees with the hypothesis! The hypothesis has a toy version!”

Papa laughed. “Models are not proof, but they help us think.”

Bear nodded. “It shows what we’re asking Webb to check.”

Captain Bolts leaned toward the foam ring. “RISS question: if it is icy, what should Webb see?”

Papa held up one blue bead. “Water ice absorbs and emits infrared light in particular ways. A spectrum can show fingerprints of ice, dust, and other molecules.”

Bear held up one gray bead. “Dust has fingerprints too.”

“Exactly,” Papa said.

Commander Aero brought the Webb request back onto the screen. The status still said: Reviewing target visibility.

Bear looked at the model. Then at the real sensor line.

“Can we send the model with the request?” he asked.

Wrenchley gasped. “To space?”

“I mean a picture,” Bear said.

Wrenchley looked relieved and a little disappointed.

Captain Bolts said, “Approved. A clear model helps humans and robots understand the question.”

So they took a picture.

Not just any picture.

Beep adjusted the lights. Wrenchley held the foam ring steady with two tool arms and tried not to block the gap with the third. Captain Bolts held a tiny label that said PROPOSED OUTER ICY BELT. Papa moved the flashlight so the two clumps glowed softly.

Bear held the notebook open beside it.

Blink-blink-pause.

Warm-warm-quiet.

Clump-clump-gap.

Beep took the image and attached it to the Webb request as a supporting diagram.

The screen updated.

Supporting materials received.

Wrenchley made a very satisfied wrench noise.

Then the screen updated again.

Target visible in limited window. Preliminary observation approved.

For one second, everybody froze.

Then everybody talked at once.

“Approved!” Bear shouted.

“Beep-beep-beep!” Beep shouted.

“Limited window means we must hurry calmly!” Captain Bolts shouted.

“I am hurrying calmly at maximum calm hurry!” Wrenchley shouted.

Papa put two fingers to his lips and gave one soft whistle.

The station quieted.

“Team,” Papa said. “We have a yes. Now we do the next careful thing.”

Commander Aero’s voice became crisp and mission-ready. “Webb can take a short infrared spectrum first. If the signal is strong enough, a brief follow-up image may be possible. We must choose what to prioritize.”

Bear stared at the screen.

The model sat beside him: clump, clump, gap.

The sensor line pulsed: warm, warm, quiet.

Far away, Webb waited in cold darkness, ready to look.

Bear felt the good kind of excitement. Big, bright, but not too big to hold.

“What do we ask Webb to do first?” he asked.

Papa smiled. “That is exactly the right question.”

What should Bear choose for Webb’s first observation? Option one: Take a spectrum first, to look for water ice fingerprints in the far signal. Option two: Take an infrared image first, to see whether the far signal has a clump-clump-gap shape. Option three: Split the short window carefully: a tiny spectrum and a tiny image, less detailed but covering both questions.

Chapter 5 — Webb Sees Two Clumps

Bear looked at the three options on the screen.

A spectrum would show what the faraway material was made of. That was important. Very important.

But Bear kept glancing at the model.

Clump. Clump. Gap.

“We already made a shape hypothesis,” Bear said. “So I think we should ask Webb to look for the shape first.”

Papa’s eyes warmed. “Tell us why.”

Bear pointed to the foam ring. “If there are not two clumps, then our ice fingerprint idea might still be true, but our model is wrong. If Webb sees two clumps, then we know the model is good enough to test next.”

Captain Bolts slowly rotated until he was right-side up. “RISS agrees. Shape first.”

Wrenchley saluted with the captured blanket pouch by mistake, then quickly saluted with an empty tool arm instead. “Shape first!”

Beep flashed blue-blue-green. “Beep!”

Commander Aero entered the command. “Requesting infrared imaging priority. Short exposure. Target lock based on RISS and Machine World combined coordinates.”

The station went into careful hurry.

Careful hurry is different from regular hurry. Regular hurry bumps into things. Careful hurry clips tools down, reads labels twice, and only bumps into things a tiny bit.

Wrenchley bumped into one handrail.

“I meant to check its sturdiness,” he said.

Papa smiled but kept working. He helped Captain Bolts confirm the station’s timing data. Bear held his notebook open beside the screen and watched the warm-warm-quiet line. Beep made the graph bigger and cleaner, with the shiny blanket flashes removed.

Now the far signal looked calmer.

Still mysterious.

But no longer messy.

Warm. Warm. Quiet.

The screen changed.

Webb observation beginning.

Bear whispered, “Hi, Webb.”

Far away, much farther than the Moon, the James Webb Space Telescope turned its golden mirror toward a tiny patch of sky. It did not zoom like a spaceship. It did not flash like a camera in a cartoon. It gathered faint infrared light, patient and cold and careful.

On the RISS, everyone waited.

Wrenchley tried to wait with dignity. His left tool arm tapped the table seventeen times.

Captain Bolts gently held the tool arm still.

“Thank you,” Wrenchley whispered.

Then the first image arrived.

At first it was not much to look at. A dark square. A few pale dots. A fuzzy smudge near the middle.

Beep processed it.

Commander Aero overlaid the Machine World coordinates.

Papa adjusted the contrast.

The fuzzy smudge sharpened.

Bear leaned forward until Papa put one steadying hand against the back of his shirt.

There it was.

A thin, cold ring around a young star.

Not a perfect ring.

Two brighter icy patches glowed on one side.

Between them and around the far edge was a dimmer gap.

Bear’s mouth opened.

Wrenchley whispered, “Clump.”

Captain Bolts whispered, “Clump.”

Beep whispered, “Beep.”

Bear pointed. “Gap.”

The model on the table and the image from Webb looked like cousins.

Not identical. Real space was messier than foam and beads. But the idea was there. Two icy clumps. One quieter stretch. A cold outer belt around a young star system, turning slowly enough that RISS sensors felt the pattern as warm-warm-quiet.

Papa’s voice was soft with pride. “Webb confirms the shape.”

Commander Aero nodded. “Two icy clumps detected. The hypothesis is strengthened.”

Bear did a very small zero-gravity fist pump. It still made him drift half an inch, so Papa caught his sleeve with two fingers.

“Tiny celebrations,” Papa said.

“Tiny but huge,” Bear said.

Wrenchley looked from the foam model to the Webb image. “Our toy version was useful.”

“Very useful,” Papa said. “That is what models are for.”

Captain Bolts pressed a button, and the station saved the image beside Bear’s diagram.

The file name appeared:

RISS-WEBB-ICY-BELT-TWO-CLUMPS-CONFIRMED.

Beep added a smaller note underneath:

NOT THE BLANKET.

Everyone agreed this was important.

Commander Aero turned toward Bear. “Chief Telescope Officer, the image gives us the shape. The next question is composition. Is it mostly water ice? Dust mixed with ice? Carbon dioxide ice? Something else?”

Bear looked at the two glowing clumps.

“They look like snowballs that forgot to become planets,” he said.

Papa chuckled. “That is not the official term, but it is a pretty good picture.”

A new message appeared from the Webb team.

Imaging successful. Limited time remains. Recommend one focused spectrum of brightest clump.

Bear looked at Papa.

Papa looked back.

“You chose well,” Papa said. “Because now we know exactly where to point the spectrum.”

Bear tapped the brighter clump on the screen.

“Then next we ask what it’s made of.”

What should Bear choose for the next step? Option one: Take a spectrum of the brightest icy clump to search for water ice fingerprints. Option two: Compare both clumps to see whether they are made of the same material. Option three: Ask RISS to watch the belt’s rhythm while Webb studies the clump, combining timing and chemistry together.

Chapter 6 — The Warmer Clump

Bear pointed at the two icy clumps on the Webb image.

“That one is brighter,” he said. “But brighter doesn’t always mean bigger, right?”

Papa’s smile grew very proud and very quiet.

“Right,” he said. “Brighter can mean bigger. Or warmer. Or shinier. Or some mixture.”

Wrenchley lifted two tool arms. “I am shiny and small.”

Captain Bolts nodded. “Confirmed.”

Beep flashed once. “Beep.”

Bear tapped the brighter clump again. “I think that one is a little warmer. Maybe it is starting to melt.”

Papa tilted his head. “In space, ice does something a little different. With very low pressure, surface ice can sometimes go straight from solid to vapor. That is called sublimation.”

Bear tried the word. “Sub-li-ma-tion.”

“Excellent,” Papa said. “But ‘melting a tiny bit’ is a perfectly good first picture. The science version is: if the clump is warmer, some ice may be changing at the surface, which can make it reflect and glow differently.”

Commander Aero’s wings gave a soft click. “Then we need a comparison measurement. Not just one clump. Both.”

Captain Bolts opened the Webb request panel. “Limited time remains. Comparison mode possible if exposure is short.”

Wrenchley leaned toward the screen. “Short exposure means more noise.”

Papa nodded. “Yes. But comparing two targets the same way can still be useful. Same instrument, same method, two clumps.”

Bear wrote in his notebook:

Question: Is bright clump warmer? Test: compare clump A and clump B. Look for: infrared thermal curve plus ice fingerprints.

Then he paused. “Papa? Does a spectroscopic line show temperature?”

“Good question,” Papa said. “A single line is more like a fingerprint. It tells us what material is there. Temperature comes from the shape of the infrared glow, and sometimes from comparing features. So Webb can help us measure both: what it is, and how warm it is.”

Bear nodded slowly. “So we ask for the fingerprints and the warmth shape.”

“That’s it.”

Commander Aero entered the request: compare the brighter icy clump and the dimmer icy clump using the same infrared settings.

The RISS grew quiet again.

This time, waiting felt different. Not like waiting for permission. Like waiting for a thermometer the size of a space telescope to finish being very careful.

On the screen, Webb gathered light from the brighter clump first.

Then the dimmer one.

Two graphs appeared side by side.

At first they looked like mountain ranges drawn by a sleepy robot.

Wrenchley brightened. “I love sleepy robot mountains.”

Beep processed the data.

The graphs smoothed.

Papa pointed. “There. Both show water ice features.”

Bear leaned closer. “So they are icy.”

“Yes.”

Commander Aero highlighted the brighter clump’s curve. “And this clump’s infrared emission peaks slightly shorter than the dimmer clump.”

Bear looked at Papa.

Papa said, “That means warmer.”

Bear’s eyes went wide. “We were right?”

“Careful science words,” Papa said, but he was smiling. “The data supports your hypothesis.”

Wrenchley spun halfway around and caught himself on a rail. “Bright clump is warmer! Bright clump is warmer! I am saying it calmly!”

Captain Bolts checked the numbers. “Temperature difference small but real.”

Beep added a tiny animation: two blue snowballs, one with a little wiggly vapor line rising off it.

Bear laughed. “Beep made a science cartoon.”

Papa pointed to a dip in the spectrum. “See this? Water ice. And here, the shape of the thermal glow. The warmer clump may have fresh exposed ice or dust that absorbs more starlight. That could make it warm a little more and brighten.”

Bear wrote fast.

Bright clump = warmer. Water ice confirmed. Maybe fresh ice/dust surface. Tiny sublimation?

Commander Aero sent the results to the Machine World sky arrays. The silver data streams behind her shifted from tangled knots into smooth ribbons.

“The model now has three supports,” Aero said. “Image: two clumps and a gap. Spectrum: water ice present. Thermal comparison: brighter clump is warmer.”

Captain Bolts made an official voice. “RISS finding: not a blanket, not an argument, not magic.”

Beep flashed proudly. “Beep.”

Wrenchley added, “And only slightly my fault at the beginning.”

Papa chuckled. “The blanket was part of the mystery. Finding it mattered.”

Bear floated beside the model, looking at the real Webb image on one screen and the graphs on another.

The universe had done something tricky. It had stacked a nearby shiny patch on top of a faraway icy belt. But they had separated the signals, tested a shape, and measured the warmth.

Bear hugged his notebook to his chest.

“I like when science is like untangling strings,” he said.

Papa nodded. “One knot at a time.”

Then Commander Aero’s screen blinked with one more message.

“Webb team requests a final label for the observation file,” she said. “A short name for the icy belt system.”

Wrenchley gasped. “Naming! The most dangerous science.”

Captain Bolts said, “The name must be clear.”

Beep said, “Beep-beep,” which sounded like, “And cute.”

Bear looked at the two clumps glowing softly on the screen.

Two icy places around a young star. One warmer than the other. A rhythm heard first as warm-warm-quiet.

He tapped his pencil against the notebook.

“What should we call it?”

What should Bear name the icy belt observation? Option one: The Warm-Warm Belt, because that is the rhythm RISS heard first. Option two: The Twin Snowclump Belt, because Webb found two icy clumps around the young star. Option three: The RISS Ice Ring, because the Robot International Space Station helped discover and understand it.

Chapter 7 — The RISS Ice Ring

Bear looked at the glowing image on the screen: the young star, the cold ring, the two brighter icy clumps, and the quieter gap between them.

“I think,” he said slowly, “we should call it the R-I-S-S Ice Ring.”

Captain Bolts tilted his head. “Spelled R-I-S-S?”

Bear nodded. “But said like Riss. Riss Ice Ring.”

Beep flashed blue, green, blue, green. “Beep-beep!”

Wrenchley pressed two tool arms to his metal chest. “The station is in the name. I am not crying oil. That would be a maintenance issue.”

Papa smiled. “RISS Ice Ring is clear. It tells where the mystery was untangled.”

Commander Aero entered the label into the observation file.

RISS ICE RING — young outer icy belt with two warmer clumps and one dimmer gap. Discovered through RISS timing data, Machine World sensor support, and James Webb infrared observations.

The words settled onto the screen.

For a moment, everybody just looked at them.

Then Captain Bolts made his official voice again, but it came out a little softer than before.

“Robot International Space Station finding complete. Nearby reflection removed. Far signal identified. Two icy clumps confirmed. Brighter clump measured warmer. Observation named by Chief Telescope Officer Bear.”

Bear’s ears warmed. “And the team.”

Captain Bolts corrected the file at once.

Named by Chief Telescope Officer Bear and the RISS investigation team.

“Better,” Bear said.

Papa gave him a proud sideways look.

Commander Aero’s silver data streams brightened behind her. “Machine World observatories will continue monitoring the RISS Ice Ring. If the warmer clump changes over time, we may learn how icy planet-building material moves and warms around young stars.”

Bear floated closer to the screen. “So it might be baby planet stuff?”

“Possibly,” Papa said. “Not a planet yet. More like leftover building material. Dust and ice and little pieces sharing an orbit.”

“Like when we build with blocks and there are extra blocks around the tower.”

“Exactly,” Papa said. “Except the blocks are icy, the tower is a possible future planet, and the room is space.”

Wrenchley raised one tool arm. “I would like to build a block tower in space.”

Captain Bolts said, “Request denied until after lunch.”

“That is not no,” Wrenchley whispered to Bear.

Bear whispered back, “It is science later.”

Beep displayed all three parts of the investigation side by side.

First: the thermal blanket patch, safely captured in its pouch.

Second: the foam model with clump, clump, gap.

Third: Webb’s infrared image of the RISS Ice Ring.

Papa pointed to them one at a time. “Near thing. Model thing. Far thing.”

Bear pointed too. “Separate. Think. Test.”

Commander Aero nodded. “A fine method.”

Captain Bolts opened a tiny compartment and took out two stickers. One said JUNIOR ORBITAL SIGNAL SCIENTIST. The other said SENIOR CALM COMPARISON ENGINEER.

Bear stuck the first one carefully on his notebook.

Papa looked at his sticker. “Senior Calm Comparison Engineer?”

Wrenchley said, “You compared calmly. It is accurate.”

Papa stuck it to his shirt.

Then Beep produced a third sticker. It was hand-printed, slightly crooked, and read: NOT THE BLANKET.

Wrenchley took that one with great dignity.

At last, Commander Aero opened the tablet bridge home. Silver pixels shimmered in the airlock doorway.

Captain Bolts saluted. Beep beeped. Wrenchley waved the captured blanket patch from inside its pouch.

“Come back for science lunch!” Wrenchley called.

“What is science lunch?” Bear asked.

“We test which foods float funniest.”

Papa said, “That sounds extremely peer-reviewed.”

Bear laughed as he and Papa stepped through the tablet bridge.

Fizzzzip.

The RISS became silver light. The silver light became the kitchen. The kitchen became cereal bowls, Bear’s pencil, and one spoon that had somehow ended up on Papa’s chair.

Bear looked at it.

Papa looked at it.

“Still breakfasty,” Bear said.

Papa picked it up. “Very mysterious.”

Bear opened his notebook to a fresh page and wrote the new name in big letters.

RISS ICE RING

Under it he drew two icy clumps, one tiny wiggly vapor line, and a little square off to the side labeled definitely blanket.

Papa sat beside him.

“You know what I liked best?” Papa asked.

“What?”

“You did not jump to the biggest answer first. You separated the nearby thing from the faraway thing.”

Bear tapped his pencil. “One knot at a time.”

“One knot at a time,” Papa said.

The science tablet chimed softly. A message from Commander Aero appeared.

RISS Ice Ring monitoring has begun. Excellent work, team.

Bear smiled.

Outside, the day was bright. Somewhere far above, the Robot International Space Station circled Earth. Far beyond that, Webb watched the cold dark with its golden mirror. And farther still, around a young star, two icy clumps turned slowly through space.

Warm.

Warm.

Quiet.

Bear closed his notebook, but not all the way. A good science page, he thought, should always be easy to open again.
