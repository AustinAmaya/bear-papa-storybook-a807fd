The Race Through the Whispering Woods

---

---

Preamble

The Whispering Woods were extra whispery that morning.

Bear was standing at the edge of the yard, hands in his pockets, watching the tall trees sway. Papa was on the deck with his coffee, reading something in a small notebook.

Then — THWUMP.

Something landed on the grass behind him. Bear turned around. It was Cheetah. She was crouched low, spotted tail flicking, golden eyes bright. She had the biggest grin Bear had ever seen on anyone.

"I challenge you," she said, "to a race."

Bear raised an eyebrow. "Through the woods?"

"Through the woods," Cheetah confirmed.

Bear looked at the trees. He looked at Cheetah. He looked at his hands.

"Okay," he said.

---

Chapter 1

Papa set down his notebook and walked over, looking very official about it.

"I'll referee," he said. "Start at the big mossy rock. Finish at the Observation Stone. First one there wins."

Bear knew the Observation Stone. He and Papa had found it together — a flat carved rock under a gap in the canopy where the Shepherd Star sat on clear nights. It was deep enough in the woods to make a proper race.

"Ready?" said Papa.

"Ready," said Bear.

"Ready," said Cheetah, already bouncing on her feet.

"Go!"

Cheetah was gone in an instant. Bear ran as fast as his legs would carry him, ducking under branches, hopping over roots. The woods smelled like moss and cool air. He ran and ran.

He arrived at the Observation Stone, panting.

Cheetah was already there. She was lying on the flat stone, one arm behind her head, looking extremely relaxed.

"Hi," she said.

"You're very fast," Bear said.

"I know," she said cheerfully.

---

Chapter 2

"Rematch," said Bear. "But this time I pick the path."

Cheetah hopped off the Observation Stone. "You can pick any path you want. I'll still win."

Papa was catching up through the trees. When he arrived, Bear explained the plan.

"I get to use whatever path I find. She has to use the main path."

Papa stroked his chin. "That seems fair. As long as both paths end at the same stone."

"They do," said Bear.

He'd remembered something. On one of their evening walks, Papa had mentioned a gap — a tight squeeze between two leaning pines that the main path curved all the way around. Bear had filed it away in the back of his mind, the way he filed things away in his blue notebook.

Cheetah didn't know about the gap. Bear did.

---

Chapter 3

Papa positioned himself at the mossy rock again.

"Ready?"

Cheetah shook out her legs. She was not worried at all. Bear stood still and breathed slowly.

"Go!"

Cheetah shot forward on the main path, a golden blur between the trees.

Bear turned left.

He pushed through a stand of ferns, found the two leaning pines exactly where he remembered them, and squeezed through the gap. The gap smelled like pine sap and cool shadow. He had to duck down and turn sideways, but he made it through and came out just ahead of the big bramble curve — the part of the main path that added a long loop before it straightened toward the Observation Stone.

Bear ran.

Cheetah burst through the tree line ahead of the bramble curve — and found Bear already there, running right beside her.

Her eyes went wide.

---

Chapter 4

They ran. Side by side, through the last stretch of trees, feet thudding on the soft forest floor.

The Observation Stone was just ahead, the familiar gap in the canopy letting in a column of afternoon light.

Bear threw everything he had into the last ten steps. His hands pumped. His sneakers hit the ground — one, two, three —

He touched the stone.

Half a second later, Cheetah's hand came down beside his.

Both of them bent over, catching their breath.

"That," panted Cheetah, "was a SHORTCUT."

"Yep," said Bear.

"Where did it — how did you even — "

"Papa showed me," Bear said. "On one of our walks."

Cheetah straightened up and looked at Bear very seriously. Then she looked at Papa, who had arrived at a calmer pace and was watching with light-brown eyes that looked pleased.

"You knew about the shortcut," she said to Papa.

"I might have mentioned it once," said Papa.

---

Chapter 5

Bear walked Cheetah back to the leaning pines.

She studied the gap. She crouched down and looked through it, then stood back up, then crouched again.

"It's so small," she said.

"You have to duck," said Bear.

"I'm a cheetah," said Cheetah. "I don't duck."

"You could belly-crawl," said Papa helpfully.

Cheetah gave Papa a look. Then, with great dignity, she got down, flattened herself, and army-crawled through the gap. She popped out the other side with pine needles on her chin.

"There," she said. "I have now used the shortcut."

Bear laughed. It was a good laugh, the kind that fills up the whole chest.

They walked back to the Observation Stone together. The afternoon light came through the canopy gap and lit up the flat carved surface. Bear put his hand on it.

"We should come back tonight," he said. "See if the Shepherd Star is out."

"Yes," said Cheetah immediately.

"Yes," said Papa, in the quiet way that meant he'd already been thinking the same thing.

The woods rustled softly all around them, the way they always did.

*The End.*
