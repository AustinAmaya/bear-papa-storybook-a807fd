The Strange Sound

---

---

Preamble

Bear heard it first.

He was in bed, which was where you were supposed to be at nine-thirty on a Tuesday evening. The window was open a little — just the screen, because the night had been warm — and the Whispering Woods were doing their usual thing. A soft sound, like breathing. Leaves. Wind from somewhere further away.

Then, underneath all of it, something else.

Bear lay very still and listened.

It was low. Lower than the wind. Lower than any animal he'd heard. Not scary — he checked himself for scared, the way Papa had taught him, and he wasn't. Just... curious. Very, very curious.

It was a hum.

He sat up.

A deep, steady hum, coming from the direction of the Whispering Woods. Regular as a clock. Warm like a cello.

Bear got out of bed.

*What should Bear do first?*

---

Chapter 1

Papa was in his study, which was what he called the room where he kept his star charts and his books and the photograph of the 1923 Shepherd Star annotation.

Bear knocked twice — their knock.

"Come in."

Papa was at his desk with a notebook open. His reading lamp made a warm circle. He looked up.

"Bear. It's nine-thirty."

"I know. Papa, there's a sound."

Papa listened. This was something Bear liked about Papa — he actually listened before asking questions. He tilted his head toward the window.

A few seconds passed.

"I hear it," Papa said.

"You do?"

"Low. Steady. From the woods."

"Yes." Bear came all the way into the study. "It's not like the usual sound. It's under it."

Papa stood up. He wasn't alarmed — his expression was the same one he got at the Observation Stone, or when he found the old map. The interested face. "Get your shoes and your vest. And your notebook."

Bear was already moving.

He paused at the door. "Should we get Cheetah?"

Papa was already putting on his jacket. "I think Cheetah would never forgive us if we didn't."

*How should they get Cheetah's attention?*

---

Chapter 2

Bear stood at the edge of the yard, near the Threshold, and called — not a shout, just the right volume. The kind of call that carried.

"Cheetah."

A pause.

"Cheetah."

From somewhere in the darkness past the tree line, something moved. Then: THWUMP. Cheetah hit Bear in the chest and he stumbled backward against Papa, who was solid and didn't move.

"You called," said Cheetah.

"I did," Bear said, from under her chin.

"I was nearby." She released him and looked at Papa. "What's happening? I can feel something. Under my feet. Is that—"

"We're investigating," Papa said.

Cheetah's ears went forward. "I'm ready."

The three of them stood still in the yard and listened. The hum came through clearly now — low, regular, from somewhere deep in the woods. Definitely not wind. Not water. Not an animal Bear knew.

"Cheetah," Bear said. "Do you know what it is?"

"No. But I can tell where it's coming from." She turned slightly. "That direction. Past the Pebble Ground, I think. Maybe past the Great Old Tree."

Bear opened his notebook. He had his map of the Whispering Woods — the three original paths, and the new one he'd added after finding the old map. He studied it in the dark by the light of Papa's small flashlight.

"If it's that direction," Bear said slowly, "it might be near the Listening Stone."

Cheetah and Papa both looked at him.

"We only found that last time," Bear said. "Maybe this is why it's called the Listening Stone."

*How should they navigate the dark woods?*

---

Chapter 3

Papa took the flashlight and Bear took the notebook.

The Whispering Woods at night were different. The trees were bigger somehow, or felt bigger, the way rooms feel bigger in the dark. The path was narrow and the ferns caught the edge of the flashlight beam and turned silver.

But Bear wasn't scared. He touched the first tree at the entrance — rough bark, ordinary — and then the gap between the leaning pines was ahead.

"The shortcut," Bear said.

Cheetah was already moving. She ducked through the leaning-pine gap with considerably more grace than the first time she'd done it — no pine needles on her chin this time.

"I've been practicing," she said, on the other side.

The hum was louder inside the woods. More present. Bear could feel it in his chest now, a small vibration, like standing next to a large instrument.

"It's coming from below," Cheetah said. "Not from above. Not from any tree."

Papa crouched and pressed his hand to the path. "She's right. It's in the ground."

"The ground is humming?" Bear asked.

"The ground is carrying something that's humming," Papa said. "Sound travels through earth. Whatever is making this sound might be further away than it seems."

Bear wrote: *Hum in ground — felt through feet — not a tree, not wind, not water. Coming from deep woods direction.*

They passed the Pebble Ground. In the dark, the flat stones were pale shapes in the grass, the filtered light gone, the clearing holding still like it always held still.

Cheetah touched the stone Cheetah-pebble that marked the center.

"Still here," she said quietly.

They kept walking.

*What do they find near the Great Old Tree?*

---

Chapter 4

At the Great Old Tree, the hum was unmistakable.

Bear put his hand on the bark — thick, grooved, the anchor point of everything. He felt it immediately. Not loud. Not vibrating the tree exactly. More like the tree was resonating, the way a glass resonates when you run a wet finger around the rim.

"It's the tree," Bear said.

"It's in the tree," Papa corrected. "But the source is further. The tree is just... receiving it."

Cheetah pressed her ear against the bark. Her eyes went wide. "Oh. *Oh.* It's much louder here."

Papa looked at the hollow where the old map had been. He shone the flashlight in carefully. Empty — the oilskin package was safe in his study now.

"This direction," said Cheetah. She turned toward the northwest path — the new path Bear had drawn into his notebook. The one that led to the Listening Stone.

Bear was already moving.

The path through the deep woods was quieter. He remembered this from the last time — the way the Whispering Woods went from whisper to breath the deeper you went. And now, underneath the breath, the hum.

The Listening Stone was ahead.

They came into the clearing and Bear stopped.

The hum was loudest here. Clear and warm and deep. The ring of smaller stones were exactly as they'd left them — half-sunk, mossy, facing inward.

Bear pressed both hands flat on the Listening Stone's surface.

It was warm. Warmer than before. And he could feel the hum moving through it, through his palms, through his arms.

"Papa," he said. "What is it?"

Papa knelt at the stone and listened.

*What does Papa think is making the sound?*

---

Chapter 5

"I think," Papa said slowly, "that there is water. Underground. Deep underground. And when it moves in large amounts — maybe after rain, maybe because of something seasonal — it creates a sound. A resonance."

"The ground is singing?" Cheetah asked.

"Something like that." Papa sat back. "The person who built the Listening Stone knew this. They put it here because here, you can feel it. The stone carries the sound."

Bear thought about the Observation Stone — aligned to the sky, to the Shepherd Star. Pointing up.

The Listening Stone pointed down. Or received what came from below.

"The same person built both," Bear said. "One for the sky. One for the ground."

"Possibly."

Bear wrote in his notebook: *Listening Stone — resonates when underground water moves. Built by same hand as Observation Stone. The friend from a long time ago knew about both the sky and the ground.*

He added it to the page he'd started: *What we know about the friend from a long time ago.*

The three of them stayed by the Listening Stone for a while. The hum was steady and slow, like a pulse. The woods were dark and cool and entirely peaceful.

Cheetah lay down on the ground and spread her front paws flat on the earth.

"I can feel it through the ground," she said. "All of it. Like the whole woods is breathing."

"It is," Papa said.

Bear lay down too. He pressed both hands to the earth the same way, and felt it — the distant sound, the slow movement, the ground alive under his palms.

"Papa," he said after a while. "There are still two more paths on the old map we haven't walked."

"I know."

"And the Shepherd Star mystery."

"I know that too."

"And now this." Bear looked up at the canopy, dark branches and a few stars showing through. "The friend from a long time ago left us a lot to figure out."

Papa smiled — Bear could hear it in his voice. "Good thing we have a notebook."

They walked back through the dark Whispering Woods together, Bear with his notebook, Papa with the flashlight, Cheetah between them. The hum faded as they came back through the leaning-pine gap and into the yard.

The Threshold stood just inside the tree line, solid and good, birch trees and wattle wall, a small dark shape against the darker woods.

Little Red Bear was on the deck railing.

"Late," it observed.

"We were investigating," said Bear.

"Did you find anything?"

Bear thought about the Listening Stone warm under his hands. The whole woods breathing. The friend from a long time ago who had known enough to mark it.

"We found something good," he said.

He tucked his notebook into his vest pocket and went inside.

*The End.*
