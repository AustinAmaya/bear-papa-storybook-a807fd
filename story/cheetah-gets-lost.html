Cheetah Gets Lost

---

---

Preamble

It started with a butterfly.

At least, Bear was pretty sure it was a butterfly. He didn't get a good look, because the instant it fluttered past the edge of the Whispering Woods, Cheetah was gone.

One second she was standing next to Bear, hands on her knees, looking at something small and bright. The next second: gone. A flash of spotted tail between the trees.

Bear counted to ten. Then to twenty.

Cheetah did not come back out.

Papa appeared on the deck. "Where did Cheetah go?"

"In," said Bear.

Papa looked at the woods. The woods rustled softly the way they always did.

"How long ago?"

"About thirty seconds," Bear said.

Papa came down the steps. "Then let's go find her."

---

Chapter 1

Bear grabbed his blue notebook from the shelf by the door, flipped to the map page, and tucked it in his vest pocket.

The map had three paths now. The main path to the Observation Stone. The branch left at the root gap for the Quiet Creek. And the new path from two days ago — the one that curved through the gully to the Pebble Ground and then to the Great Old Tree.

He looked at the map for three seconds, then closed the notebook.

"Ready," he said.

They stepped into the Whispering Woods.

Bear called out: "Cheetah!"

The trees absorbed the sound. Far off, something moved — a branch, probably a bird — but no answer.

Papa stayed close. His dark-brown fur was calm. "She can take care of herself," he said. "She's very fast."

"That's the problem," said Bear. "She's really fast."

---

Chapter 2

Bear followed the new path.

It curved around the root systems, dipped into the shallow gully, and came back up. Bear recognized every turn — he'd mapped it two days ago. He kept his ears open.

At the entrance to the Pebble Ground, he stopped and looked. The clearing was empty. The flat stone sat quietly in the light, the little depression where the white pebble had been still faintly visible. Bear patted his jacket pocket. Pebble still there.

"She came through here," he said. He pointed to a fern at the clearing's edge, bent back the wrong way — something large had pushed through fast.

"Good eyes," Papa said.

"She went past the birch trees," Bear said. "Past where the path ends."

He looked at the two close-growing birch trees at the back of the clearing. The path officially ended there. But beyond them the woods continued — and that was where the path became the Great Old Tree, and past that was the deeper, quieter part, where the trees grew denser and the sounds changed.

"Carefully," said Papa.

"I know," said Bear.

---

Chapter 3

They came through the birch trees to the Great Old Tree.

Bear put his hand on the bark. Thick. Deeply grooved, like the folds of something ancient. The Great Old Tree was the biggest, oldest, most impossible-to-miss thing in the Whispering Woods. Papa had told him once: if you can find one thing you know, you can find everything else.

"From here," Bear said, "the deep woods are that direction." He pointed. "The Quiet Creek is — " He thought for a moment, checking the map in his head. "That way. Past the root gap."

Papa nodded.

Bear cupped his hands around his mouth. "CHEETAH!"

Silence. Then — distant, but clear — a voice.

"BEAR."

It came from the left. Not the Quiet Creek direction. Somewhere between, in the deeper part of the woods where the humming was lower and quieter than anywhere else.

"She's not lost-lost," Papa said, quietly. "She answered."

"Come on," said Bear, and he moved carefully between the trees toward the sound.

---

Chapter 4

"CHEETAH!"

"BEAR!"

Closer.

"CHEETAH, KEEP TALKING!"

"I AM TALKING. I'M RIGHT HERE."

"WHERE IS HERE?"

A pause. Then, smaller: "There's a big mossy log. And the trees are very close together. And I don't know which way is back."

Bear looked at Papa. Papa looked at Bear. They were maybe thirty steps from the Great Old Tree — Bear could still see its bulk through the trees.

"Can you see a big tree?" Bear called. "A really big one, wider than our house?"

Another pause.

"Maybe? There's something — yes. I see a really wide trunk."

"That's the Great Old Tree," Bear called. "Walk toward it. Slowly."

Silence. Then the sound of movement, leaves and branches. Then more silence. Bear stood very still and waited.

A spotted face appeared between two pine trunks, looking mildly embarrassed.

"Hi," said Cheetah.

"Hi," said Bear.

Papa let out a long, very slow breath, the kind that wasn't quite a sigh.

---

Chapter 5

They walked back together, all three of them, Cheetah between Bear and Papa.

At the Great Old Tree, Bear stopped and put his hand on the bark again. He looked at Cheetah.

"Papa told me this. If you can find one thing you know, you can find everything else."

Cheetah studied the tree. "So this is the one thing."

"In the deep woods, yeah. From here you can get to the Pebble Ground, the Quiet Creek, the Observation Stone — all of it." Bear opened his blue notebook and showed her the map. "See? Everything connects to the Great Old Tree."

Cheetah looked at the map for a long moment. She traced the paths with one finger, very carefully.

"I want a copy," she said.

"I'll make you one," Bear said.

They came out of the woods into the yard in the late afternoon. The deck was there, and the house, and Little Red Bear on his perch. The woods rustled behind them, low and steady.

"You're very good at finding things," Cheetah told Bear.

Bear shrugged. "Papa taught me."

Papa, walking just behind them, said nothing. But his eyes were pleased.

*The End.*
