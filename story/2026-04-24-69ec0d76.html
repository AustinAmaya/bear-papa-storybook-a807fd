Bear, Papa, and the Stripey Gray Kitty

At bedtime, when the sky was turning blueberry-dark, Bear and Papa walked down the sidewalk with a little paper bag of warm rolls from the bakery.

Bear held the bag carefully with two hands.

“Important cargo,” Papa said.

“Extremely important,” Bear said. “Rolls are basically treasure.”

Then something went, “Mrrp?”

Bear stopped.

Beside the library steps sat a gray kitty with white stripes, neat as paintbrush lines. The kitty had bright green eyes and a tiny bell on a blue collar. It looked at Bear. It looked at Papa. Then it stood up, swished its tail, and trotted away.

Bear blinked. “Papa?”

Papa knelt to Bear’s eye level. “That kitty seems friendly. And very certain.”

The kitty looked back and gave another tiny, bossy “Mrrp!”

“I think it wants us to follow,” Bear whispered.

So they did.

The kitty led them past the library, around the flower pots, and toward the little park. It did not run too fast. Every few steps, it waited, as if checking that Bear and Papa were using their legs correctly.

At the park gate, Little Polar Bear stood on his stone block, watching over the neighborhood like always. Moonlight shone on his nose.

The gray kitty hopped onto the path and pawed at something shiny in the grass.

Bear crouched. “It’s a gear!”

Papa picked it up. “A little brass gear. From a machine.”

Just then, the town clock above the park pavilion went, “Bong… bong… bonk… clunk.”

The clock hands spun backward.

“Oh dear,” Papa said.

Bear’s eyes grew round. “That clock tells everyone when it’s bedtime.”

“And if it keeps spinning,” Papa said, “the bakery might open at midnight and the pigeons will think it is breakfast.”

“That would be chaos,” Bear said seriously.

The kitty sneezed, then trotted toward the pavilion.

Inside, the clock’s back panel hung open. Springs wiggled. Wheels clicked. One important gear was missing.

Bear held up the brass gear. “This one?”

Papa smiled. “Good noticing.”

Bear puffed his chest a little. He liked noticing.

But the gear slot was high up, behind a tangle of tick-tock parts. Bear could not reach it. Papa could reach, but his big paw did not fit.

The gray kitty leapt onto a bench, then onto a low shelf, then onto a narrow ledge.

“Careful, little friend,” Bear said.

The kitty tapped a wooden handle with one paw.

A small ladder folded down from the wall.

Papa laughed softly. “A clever kitty led us to a clever ladder.”

Bear climbed with Papa right behind him, one hand near Bear’s back but not pushing. Bear liked doing things himself. He also liked knowing Papa was there.

At the top, Bear looked inside the clock.

“So many wheels,” he said.

“Gears,” Papa said. “When one turns, its teeth push the next one. Like friends passing a message.”

Bear turned the brass gear in his fingers. “So if this friend is missing…”

“The message gets muddled.”

The clock suddenly went CLANG.

The minute hand spun so fast it made a windy whoosh. The rolls bag puffed open. One roll bounced out, rolled across the floor, and landed beside the kitty.

The kitty sniffed it politely and did not eat it.

“Very good manners,” Papa said.

Bear frowned at the clock. “The gears are going too fast.”

Papa pointed to a little swinging piece. “That is the brake. It slows things down. But it’s stuck.”

Bear took a slow breath, the kind Papa had taught him. In through the nose. Out like cooling soup.

“I can fix the message,” Bear said.

“I believe you,” Papa said.

Bear reached in carefully. He moved the stuck brake. Tick… tick… tick. The clock slowed. Then he slid the brass gear into its spot.

For one quiet second, nothing happened.

Then the gears began to turn together.

Tick.

Tock.

Tick.

Tock.

The clock hands spun forward, then settled gently on bedtime.

Outside, Little Polar Bear’s stone shadow seemed to nod.

The kitty gave a pleased “Mrrp!” and hopped down.

But as Bear climbed down, the clock made one last tiny sound.

Ping!

A small silver screw bounced away and rolled through a crack in the floorboards.

“Oh no,” Bear said.

Papa rubbed his chin. “The clock is fixed, but it may wobble without that screw.”

The gray kitty stretched one paw under the floorboard crack. Too short.

Bear looked around. “We need a tool.”

Papa held up the bakery twist-tie from the roll bag. “A bendy wire.”

Bear grinned. “A tiny fishing hook!”

Together, they bent the twist-tie into a little loop. Bear lay on his tummy, reached it through the crack, and fished slowly. The kitty lay beside him, whiskers forward, watching like a furry inspector.

“Little more left,” Papa said.

Bear moved left.

“Now pull back.”

Bear pulled.

The silver screw popped out.

“Yes!” Bear whispered, because the park felt sleepy now.

Papa tightened the screw. The clock ticked steady and calm.

The kitty rubbed its cheek against Bear’s shoe.

Bear read the tag on its collar. “Her name is Misty.”

On the back was an address: Library Cottage.

“That is just behind the library,” Papa said.

Misty walked them there proudly, tail high. At the cottage door, an elderly librarian opened up in slippers.

“Misty!” she said. “There you are, my stripey helper.”

Bear told her about the clock, the gear, the ladder, and the screw. Papa added only the parts Bear forgot, which was not many.

The librarian smiled. “Misty always knows when something needs mending.”

Misty blinked slowly, as if that was obvious.

Soon Bear and Papa walked home under quiet stars. Little Red Bear sat happily retired on the deck, looking cozy in the porch light.

Inside, Papa warmed the rolls. Bear ate one with butter while wearing pajamas.

“Gears pass the message,” Bear murmured.

“They do,” Papa said. “And helpers do too.”

Bear smiled sleepily. “Misty passed the message to us.”

Papa tucked the blanket around him. “And you listened.”

Bear’s eyes drifted closed. The town clock ticked far away, steady and soft, telling the whole neighborhood it was time to rest.

Tick.

Tock.

Bear was safe. Papa was near. And all the little gears of the night turned just right.
