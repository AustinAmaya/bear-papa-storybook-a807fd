Bear and Papa Return to Captain Pippa’s Crew Station

Preamble

Bear was in pajamas again.

The telescope sat on the deck like it was trying very hard to look ordinary.

Little Red Bear rested happily on the rail. Little Polar Bear watched the garden path, bright and quiet in the moonlight.

Papa knelt beside Bear and whispered, “Same crew station?”

Bear nodded. “Same Captain Pippa.”

The silver button blinked once.

The deck shimmered. The stars made a soft whoosh. This time, Bear and Papa did not appear near the ground, or a hallway, or a snack drawer.

They appeared floating right beside Captain Pippa.

She grinned. “Perfect timing, crew.”

What should tonight’s mission be?

1. The Sleepy Robot Arm — the station’s helper arm keeps waving very slowly at the moon. 2. The Missing Moon Sock — Captain Pippa’s lucky space sock has floated into a mystery place. 3. The Twinkle Alarm — a gentle alarm keeps pinging, but nobody knows why.

Wonderful choice. The twinkle alarm is softly pinging, and Captain Pippa needs her best cozy crew.

Chapter 1: The Twinkle That Would Not Stop

Ping.

A tiny light blinked above Captain Pippa’s shoulder.

Ping.

Bear floated beside Papa, his pajama knees drifting up toward his tummy.

“Papa,” Bear whispered, “the space station is saying hello.”

Captain Pippa shook her spotted head. Her blue astronaut vest had three shiny patches and one cracker crumb stuck to it.

“That,” she said, “is the Twinkle Alarm.”

Ping.

Bear looked at the little light. It was not red. It was not loud. It was a soft silver-blue, like a star blinking through a window.

“It sounds polite,” Bear said.

“It is polite,” said Pippa. “But it has been polite forty-seven times.”

Papa held a handrail with one paw and gently tucked Bear’s sleeve so he would not float sideways.

“What does the Twinkle Alarm usually mean?” Papa asked.

Captain Pippa pointed down the round hallway. “It means something small is sparkling where it should not sparkle.”

Bear’s eyes got big. “A space jewel?”

“Maybe,” said Pippa.

“A tiny comet?”

“Possibly.”

“A very shiny raisin?”

Captain Pippa looked serious. “That happened once.”

Ping.

The light blinked again.

Pippa pulled a little tablet from a Velcro patch on the wall. The screen showed a wiggly map of the crew station. One room blinked with a tiny star shape.

“The alarm says the sparkle is near the observation window,” Pippa said. “But every time I go look, it disappears.”

Bear crossed his arms, which made him slowly turn upside down.

Papa caught his ankle.

“Thank you,” Bear said. “My thinking got spinny.”

Papa smiled. “In microgravity, even thinking can use a handrail.”

Captain Pippa pushed gently toward the hallway. “Crew, we need to investigate. If the sparkle is just a shiny wrapper, fine. If it is a loose tool, it could float into something important.”

Bear nodded. “Small things matter in space.”

“They do,” Papa said.

Ping.

Bear took one careful breath.

“Captain Pippa,” he said, “we are ready for twinkle duty.”

Captain Pippa saluted. The salute made her drift backward into a cushion.

“Excellent,” she said. “Mostly.”

### What should Bear, Papa, and Captain Pippa do first?

1. Follow the blinking map straight to the observation window. 2. Use Pippa’s sparkle scanner to search slowly and carefully. 3. Check the air vents in case the sparkle is being blown around.

Excellent. Slow and careful is the very best way to find a sneaky twinkle.

Chapter 2: The Sparkle Scanner

Captain Pippa unhooked a small gadget from the wall.

It looked like a flashlight, a magnifying glass, and a tiny soup spoon had built a robot together.

“This,” she said proudly, “is the Sparkle Scanner.”

Bear reached for it.

Captain Pippa held up one finger. “Scanner rule.”

Bear stopped his paw in midair. It floated there politely.

“In space,” Pippa said, “we do not wave tools around quickly. Tools get ideas.”

Papa nodded. “Slow hands. Gentle pushes.”

Bear nodded too. “No tool ideas.”

Pippa handed him the scanner. It had a soft handle and a little screen with three dots.

Papa placed one paw on the handrail beside Bear. “Try moving it like you are painting the air with moonlight.”

Bear smiled. “That is slow.”

He clicked the scanner on.

Beep.

A pale blue line shone across the wall.

The three of them floated down the hallway. Captain Pippa went first, quick but careful. Papa stayed near Bear. Bear held the scanner out in front.

The hallway hummed softly.

On the walls were straps, handrails, labels, little Velcro patches, and bags clipped safely in place. Everything had a home.

Mostly.

Bear moved the scanner past a sleeping bag.

Nothing.

Past a packet labeled TOMATO SOUP.

Nothing.

Past a tiny photograph stuck to the wall.

Bip.

Bear froze.

“The scanner said bip,” he whispered.

Captain Pippa floated backward so fast her tail made a comma shape.

“Where?” she asked.

Bear pointed the scanner at the photograph again.

Bip.

Papa leaned close. “Look carefully.”

Bear looked. The photograph had a shiny corner. The scanner was noticing that.

“False twinkle,” Bear said.

“Very scientific,” Papa said. “A false signal means the tool found something real, but not the thing we need.”

Captain Pippa tapped the tablet. “The Twinkle Alarm is still pointing near the observation window.”

Ping.

The alarm blinked.

Bear moved on.

This time he scanned the ceiling, then the floor, though in space the floor was mostly just another wall with opinions.

They reached the observation window.

Earth glowed below them, blue and white and soft. Bear wanted to stare at it for a long time, but the alarm went—

Ping.

The scanner answered.

Bip-bip.

Captain Pippa’s ears perked. “That is not a photograph bip.”

Bear held very still.

Papa whispered, “What do you notice?”

Bear narrowed his blue eyes.

A tiny sparkle flashed near the window.

Then it vanished.

Then it flashed again, farther away.

“It moved,” Bear said.

Captain Pippa frowned gently. “Sparkles should not play hide-and-seek during safety checks.”

Bear scanned slowly.

Bip-bip-bip.

A tiny silver fleck drifted out from behind a soft equipment pouch. It was no bigger than a cornflake, but it glittered like a star that had forgotten bedtime.

“There it is!” Bear said.

Captain Pippa reached for a net clipped to her vest.

But the air vent gave a soft whoosh.

The silver fleck twirled away.

It slipped toward a row of small science boxes, each one blinking green.

Pippa gasped. “Those are my plant experiment monitors. If the fleck floats behind them, we may lose it again.”

Bear hugged the scanner carefully to his chest.

Papa’s voice stayed calm. “Then we make a plan before we push.”

Bear took a slow breath.

Slow hands. Gentle pushes. Notice first.

The fleck twinkled once more.

Ping.

### What should the crew use to catch the tiny silver fleck?

1. Captain Pippa’s soft net, moving very slowly from the side. 2. A gentle puff of air, to guide it away from the science boxes. 3. A sticky Velcro patch on a grabber, so the fleck can cling safely.

Perfect choice. Bear picks the sticky Velcro grabber, because tiny twinkles need tiny traps.

Chapter 3: The Tiny Star That Would Not Sit Still

Captain Pippa opened a wall pouch labeled CAREFUL CATCHING THINGS.

Inside were three grabbers, two soft clips, one roll of tape, and a little bag of Velcro patches.

Bear pointed. “That one.”

It was a short silver grabber with a soft handle and a flat end. Papa peeled a fuzzy Velcro patch from the little bag.

“Fuzzy side?” Bear asked.

“Good noticing,” Papa said. “Velcro works because one side has tiny hooks, and the other side has tiny loops. The hooks grab the loops.”

Bear looked closely. “Like a hundred tiny hands saying, ‘Got you.’”

Captain Pippa nodded. “Very clingy tiny hands.”

Papa stuck the fuzzy patch to the flat end of the grabber.

Bear held the sparkle scanner in one paw and the grabber in the other. Then his feet drifted upward.

“Papa,” Bear said, “my feet are trying to be ideas again.”

Papa gently held Bear’s pajama cuff. “We’ll anchor you.”

Captain Pippa clipped a soft strap from Bear’s sleeve to a handrail. It was loose enough for him to move, but snug enough that he would not float away.

“Crew rule,” Pippa said. “Before catching a thing, catch yourself.”

Bear nodded seriously. “I am caught.”

The tiny silver fleck twinkled near the plant experiment monitors.

Ping.

The Twinkle Alarm blinked.

Bip-bip-bip.

The scanner agreed.

Captain Pippa squinted at the fleck. “It’s moving toward the monitor fan.”

The fan was small. It made a quiet whirring sound, moving air over the plant boxes. The plant boxes were full of tiny green leaves, sleeping under purple grow lights.

Bear whispered, “We don’t want it to go in there.”

“No,” Papa said calmly. “But we have time to be careful.”

Captain Pippa checked her tablet. “A little time. The fan will pull it closer when it cycles up.”

“How long?” Bear asked.

Pippa looked at the blinking number.

“Thirty slow seconds.”

Bear’s tummy made a tiny flip.

Papa noticed. He always noticed.

“Name it,” Papa said softly.

Bear swallowed. “I feel worried.”

“That makes sense,” Papa said. “This is important. We’ll go slow together.”

Bear breathed in through his nose.

One.

Two.

Three.

Then out.

One.

Two.

Three.

His tummy felt less like a bouncing ball.

“Ready,” Bear said.

Captain Pippa floated to the left side of the plant boxes. Papa stayed by Bear’s shoulder. Bear lifted the grabber.

The fleck flashed.

Bear moved the Velcro toward it.

Slowly.

Slowly.

The fleck drifted away.

“Oh, crumb crackers,” Pippa whispered.

Bear stopped his paw. “It saw me.”

Papa smiled. “It did not see you. But your grabber moved air. Even gentle tools can push a tiny thing.”

Bear stared at the fleck.

“So the grabber makes a little invisible breeze?”

“Exactly,” Papa said. “When something is very light, tiny air movements matter.”

Captain Pippa tapped the side of her helmet cap. “Then we do not chase the twinkle.”

Bear’s eyes brightened. “We let it come to us.”

Papa’s eyes twinkled too. “That sounds like engineering.”

Captain Pippa grinned. “Crew, new plan. Bear, hold the Velcro still. Papa, block the big drift with your notebook. I will turn the fan cycle down one notch.”

She tapped the monitor panel.

The whirring softened.

The fleck slowed.

Papa pulled a small notebook from his pocket and held it like a tiny wall. He did not touch the fleck. He only stopped the air from nudging it toward the fan.

Bear held the grabber very still.

His arm wanted to wiggle.

His fingers wanted to squeeze.

His nose wanted to sneeze.

“Not now, nose,” Bear whispered.

The silver fleck spun once.

It drifted closer.

And closer.

Ping.

Bip-bip-bip-bip.

Captain Pippa whispered, “Easy, crew.”

Bear did not poke.

He did not chase.

He waited.

The fleck brushed the Velcro.

For one tiny moment, nothing happened.

Then—

Tick.

It stuck.

Bear’s mouth popped open.

“I got the star!”

Papa smiled. “You caught the reflector fleck.”

Captain Pippa pushed off the wall in exment, forgot to hold the handrail, and did a slow backward somersault.

“Professional celebration,” she said from upside down.

The Twinkle Alarm blinked once.

Ping.

Then it went quiet.

All three of them listened.

No ping.

No bip.

No whirr-worry.

Just the soft hum of the station.

Captain Pippa floated close and inspected the fleck on the Velcro. “Aha. This came from a little shiny label on the emergency flashlight case.”

Bear looked at the flashlight case clipped to the wall. One corner of its label was missing.

“So the alarm saw the sparkle,” Bear said.

“Yes,” said Pippa. “The Twinkle Alarm watches for loose shiny pieces. Shiny things can be hard to find in a space station full of lights.”

Papa nodded. “And tiny things can become big problems if they get into the wrong place.”

Bear looked at the little fleck. It seemed much less like a star now. More like a tired sticker crumb.

Captain Pippa placed it inside a clear pouch and sealed it.

“Evidence pouch,” she said. “Very official.”

Bear saluted. This time he held the handrail first, so he did not spin.

Captain Pippa saluted back.

Papa saluted too, but his notebook floated away behind him.

Bear caught it with the grabber.

Tick.

Papa laughed softly. “Thank you, Engineer Bear.”

Bear smiled. “The tiny hands got it.”

Captain Pippa checked her tablet. “Twinkle Alarm resolved. Plant monitors safe. Emergency flashlight case needs a new label.”

She looked at Bear and Papa.

“Crew, we have one more job before bedtime orbit can be peaceful.”

### What should the crew do next?

1. Repair the flashlight label so no more shiny flecks come loose. 2. Check the plant experiment monitors to make sure the tiny leaves are cozy. 3. Clean up the catching tools so nothing else floats away.

Good choice. A new label will make the flashlight safe and tidy again.

Chapter 4: The Better Label

Captain Pippa pulled out a small roll of dull gray space tape.

“Not shiny,” Bear noticed.

“Exactly,” Papa said. “Shiny labels can trick the alarm. Dull labels are calmer.”

Bear held the flashlight case steady with both paws. Papa peeled off the ragged old label. Captain Pippa wiped the case with a tiny cloth.

Then Bear pressed on the new label.

It said:

EMERGENCY FLASHLIGHT CLIPPED HOME

Bear smoothed the corners very carefully.

“No flappy bits,” he said.

Captain Pippa tested it with one gentle rub.

Nothing peeled.

Papa tested it with one tiny tug.

Still nothing peeled.

The Twinkle Alarm stayed quiet.

Captain Pippa smiled. “Crew station bedtime is officially un-twinkled.”

But then Papa’s notebook drifted past again.

Bear pointed. “Papa. Your notebook is not clipped home.”

Papa blinked. “Ah. My notebook is being a lesson.”

Bear clipped it to a Velcro patch.

Tick.

“Clipped home,” Bear said.

Captain Pippa gave him a soft high-five, which made them both float back just a little.

### How should the story end?

1. Look out the big window at Earth before going home. 2. Say goodnight to Captain Pippa and the plant leaves before going home. 3. Do one final quiet station check together.

Lovely choice. A quiet station check and soft goodnights will bring everything safely home.

Chapter 5: Clipped Home

Captain Pippa dimmed the hallway lights to bedtime blue.

“Final quiet check,” she whispered.

Bear held the sparkle scanner, but this time it did not beep. Papa floated beside him with one paw on the handrail.

They checked the flashlight.

“Clipped home,” Bear said.

They checked the notebook.

“Clipped home,” Papa said.

They checked the plant monitors. The tiny green leaves rested under their soft purple lights.

“Cozy home,” Captain Pippa whispered.

The Twinkle Alarm stayed silent.

Bear smiled. “It only wanted everything safe.”

“That is what good alarms do,” Papa said. “They help us notice.”

Captain Pippa bowed gently, which made her drift upward a little. “Thank you, Engineer Bear. Thank you, Papa. Crew station bedtime is peaceful again.”

Bear gave her the softest high-five.

“Goodnight, Captain Pippa.”

“Goodnight, Bear,” she said. “Goodnight, Papa.”

The silver button shimmered.

The space station hummed like a sleepy refrigerator. The stars blurred softly. Then the deck was under Bear’s pajama feet again.

Little Red Bear sat happily on the rail. Little Polar Bear watched the quiet garden path.

Papa carried Bear inside and tucked him into bed.

Bear murmured, “Tiny things matter.”

Papa sat close. “They do. And slow, careful helpers matter too.”

Bear’s eyes closed.

Outside, the night was still.

Inside, everything was clipped home.
