Cheetah in the Deep Woods

---

---

Preamble

It was Cheetah's idea.

"Show me the Bowl," she said.

She had arrived at Papa Bear Home the way she always did — THWUMP, out of nowhere, landing in the yard with her spotted tail high and her golden eyes bright.

"It's DOWN the new path," Bear said. "It's a little confusing."

"I can handle confusing," Cheetah said.

Bear looked at Papa, who was on the deck reading. Papa looked up.

"Want company?" Papa asked.

"We're fine," Cheetah said very fast.

Papa looked at Bear. Bear looked at Cheetah.

"We might need the map," Bear said.

"You have a map?" Cheetah said. "Show me the map."

Bear got his blue notebook. He showed Cheetah the page he'd drawn — the yard, the treeline, the new path curving left, the Great Old Tree, then the Bowl. The little circle for the pebble.

"Simple," Cheetah said.

"The path curves a lot," Bear said.

"I'll be fine," Cheetah said, already walking toward the treeline.

---

Chapter 1

The new path was just as narrow as Bear remembered.

Cheetah didn't mind the narrow parts. She was built for squeezing through things. She moved faster than Bear did, weaving between roots and branches, tail sweeping behind her.

The problem was that Cheetah moved faster than she thought.

"This is the part where the path curves," Bear called.

"Got it," Cheetah called from ahead, already around the curve.

"There's a tree—"

"I see it! Big one!"

"That's the Great Old Tree. We go *right* of it—"

But Cheetah's voice came from somewhere that wasn't right of the Great Old Tree.

Bear stopped.

"Cheetah?"

Silence.

Then, from somewhere deeper in the woods, further than Bear had ever been: "...Oh."

"Oh?" Bear called. "'Oh' what?"

A longer silence. "Bear?"

"What?"

"The path split."

Bear got to the Great Old Tree and put his hand on its bark — that thick, grooved, ancient bark. He looked to the right: the proper path toward the Bowl. He looked further right, slightly: nothing. He looked left: a narrow gap between roots.

"Did you go LEFT?" Bear called.

"...Maybe," Cheetah called back.

---

Chapter 2

Bear marked his place with his thumb in the blue notebook, folded the map page open, and went left.

The path — if it was a path — was thinner than the new path. Not a water-path this time. More like an animal path, winding between roots and low branches. The light was dimmer here. The trees were closer together, and older-looking, their bark darker and moister.

The Whispering Woods were whispering more loudly here.

Bear had always wondered if the sound changed based on where you were. It did. Deeper in, the sound was lower. More like a hum than a whisper.

He followed the signs of Cheetah: a spot of flattened moss, a bent fern, a small scuff in the soft earth where she'd skidded.

"CHEETAH!" Bear called.

"BEAR!" Cheetah's voice came from ahead and to the right. She didn't sound scared. She sounded like herself — exuberant, even. "I found something!"

"Are you okay?"

"COMPLETELY. Come look at this."

Bear followed her voice through two more root bends and a low arch of branches.

And there was Cheetah.

She was sitting in a patch of sunlight that came down through a gap in the trees. She had her paws — her hands — pressed together and was looking at something with intense focus.

A creek. Very small. Just a thread of water over pale stones.

"There's a creek," Cheetah said. "In the Whispering Woods."

"I didn't know that," Bear said.

"Neither did I," said Cheetah. "Isn't it wonderful?"

---

Chapter 3

Bear sat beside Cheetah and opened his blue notebook.

He added to his map: past the Great Old Tree, left at the root gap, through the low branches — and then a small blue line. A creek.

"What should we call it?" Cheetah asked.

Bear thought. The creek was very quiet. Just the sound of water moving over stones. Combined with the deeper hum of the Whispering Woods, it made everything feel very still.

"The Quiet Creek," Bear said.

"Perfect," Cheetah said immediately. Then she added: "Can I name something someday?"

"The Bowl was mine," Bear said, "so yes."

Cheetah looked satisfied.

Bear looked at his notebook. He could see the map growing — the yard, the main path to the Observation Stone, the new path to the Bowl, and now this branch path to the Quiet Creek. The woods were bigger than he'd thought.

"Cheetah," Bear said, "do you know the way back?"

Cheetah's tail flicked. She looked at the trees around her. She looked back the way they'd come. She looked at a different direction entirely.

"...No," she said.

"Okay," Bear said. He didn't say it in a scared way. He said it the way Papa said things: steady, like having a problem just meant you hadn't solved it yet. "I have the map."

---

Chapter 4

The map worked.

Well — the map plus Bear's memory of the tree bark and the root shapes. The map told him the general shape. His hands on the trees told him which tree was which.

The Great Old Tree was the anchor. Once Bear's hands found that bark — thick, deeply grooved, nothing else like it — he knew exactly where he was.

"Here," he said. "This way."

Cheetah padded alongside him now, not ahead. She kept her tail low, which was how Bear could tell she'd been a little worried, even if she hadn't said so.

"You always know where you are," Cheetah said.

"Papa taught me," Bear said. "He says if you can find one thing you know, you can find everything else."

"That's a Papa thing to say," Cheetah said.

"It really is," Bear agreed.

They came out at the Bowl and Cheetah stopped.

"Oh," she said, in a completely different way than before. "*This* is the Bowl?"

"Yes."

She looked at the bowl-shaped clearing, the tree roots curving like fingers, the orange mushrooms on the flat rock shelf. She walked in slowly. She crouched at the root hollow and looked at the pebble — white, with its gray stripe.

"Who left that?" she asked.

"I did," Bear said. "To say we found it and wanted to remember."

Cheetah was quiet for a moment. She touched the pebble very gently with one claw.

"That's really nice, Bear," she said. She wasn't being funny. She meant it.

---

Chapter 5

When they came out of the woods, Papa was still on the deck reading.

He didn't look up right away. Just waited.

"We found a creek," Bear announced.

Papa set down his book. "Did you."

"I got slightly lost," Cheetah admitted. "Very briefly."

"And Bear found you," Papa said.

"Bear found me," Cheetah said cheerfully. "He used the notebook map."

Papa looked at Bear with that steady, warm expression. The one that didn't make a big thing out of something but still let Bear know it mattered.

"I added the creek to the map," Bear said. "I called it the Quiet Creek."

"I wanted to name something," Cheetah added, "but Bear called dibs on the creek. So next time."

Little Red Bear spoke from the railing. "Did you see the pebble, Cheetah?"

"I did," Cheetah said. She sat down on the deck steps with a satisfied thump. "It's exactly right."

Papa brought out three glasses of cold water and they all sat on the deck while the Whispering Woods did their quiet whispering behind them. Cheetah's tail swished back and forth.

Bear opened his notebook and looked at the map. The woods were bigger now, on paper, than they'd seemed before. There was the path to the Observation Stone. The new path to the Bowl. The branch to the Quiet Creek.

And deeper in — the places they hadn't been yet.

Bear wrote at the bottom of the page: *The map is not finished.*

*The End.*
