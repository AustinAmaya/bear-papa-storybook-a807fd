Bear and Papa and the Blocky Night

Bear and Papa blinked and found themselves standing in the Minecraft world.

Everything was square.

Square trees. Square clouds. Square sheep chewing square grass.

Bear looked down at his little blocky boots and laughed. “Papa! Even my shoelaces are cubes.”

Papa knelt to Bear’s eye level, smiling with his warm light-brown eyes. “That is extremely efficient world design.”

A black cat wound around Bear’s legs. Its green eyes shone like tiny lanterns. It wore a red collar and gave a proud little meow.

“Our friend came too!” said Bear, scooping the tame black cat into a cuddle.

The cat purred like a tiny engine.

Ahead of them stood a cozy oak house with torchlight glowing in the windows. Behind it, a vegetable garden waved beside a pond. It looked safe and friendly.

Then the sky began to dim.

Bear pointed. “Papa. Night is coming.”

And far away, from the hills, came the low, shuffling sound of trouble.

Papa looked around calmly. “In Minecraft, nighttime means we should prepare before we panic.”

Bear nodded fast. He liked that plan.

They hurried to work. Bear gathered wood from a tree trunk with careful thumps. Papa shaped the wood into planks and made a crafting table. The black cat trotted beside them, flicking its tail and meowing whenever Bear wandered too close to a dark patch.

“It’s helping,” said Bear.

Papa examined the cat. “Very sensibly. It appears our friend is an excellent safety officer.”

Bear grinned. “A fuzzy one.”

Soon they had a door, extra torches, and a little fence around the garden. Papa showed Bear how torches pushed the darkness back.

“Why does that help so much?” Bear asked.

Papa set one torch on a post. Golden light bloomed over the grass. “Because light changes what can happen here. Some nighttime trouble prefers dark places. So we do not fight darkness everywhere. We make bright, safe spaces on purpose.”

Bear liked that. It felt like science and magic together.

A hiss floated from behind the pond.

Bear froze. “Papa?”

Papa put one steady hand on Bear’s shoulder. “Breathe in. One, two, three. Out. One, two, three.”

They listened.

Hiss.

A creeper bobbed into view, green and sneaky and much too close to the fence.

The black cat leaped down from Bear’s arms and sat tall, staring at it.

The creeper stopped.

It wobbled.

Then, very strangely, it backed away.

Bear’s mouth fell open. “Our cat did that!”

Papa blinked, impressed. “So the safety officer has advanced training.”

Bear giggled so hard he had to hold his tummy. Together they placed more torches while the black cat patrolled like a tiny shadow with whiskers.

By the time the moon climbed high, the house glowed warmly. The garden was safe. The pond shimmered silver. Even the square sheep looked sleepy.

Inside, Bear sat by the window with the black cat in his lap while Papa made beet soup in a crackling furnace. The room was orange and cozy.

“We were brave,” Bear said softly.

Papa handed him a bowl. “Yes. And smart. We noticed, we learned, and we made a safe place together.”

The black cat purred harder, as if it agreed.

Outside, the blocky night rustled and wandered, but inside the little house all was warm and bright. Bear ate his soup, leaned against Papa, and watched the torches shine until his eyes grew heavy.

And in the safe square quiet, with Papa near and their black cat keeping watch, Bear felt ready for sleep.
