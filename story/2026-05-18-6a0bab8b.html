**Bear and Papa in the Floating Friend Station**

Bear opened one eye.

He was not in bed.

He was floating.

Papa was floating too, slowly turning upside down with one slipper drifting beside his ear.

“Papa?” Bear whispered.

Papa blinked. “Hmm. This is not our bedroom.”

All around them was a cozy space station with round windows, twinkly buttons, and a soft hum like a sleepy refrigerator.

Then Bear heard voices.

“Help!” called Turtle.

“I’m spinning!” called George Dino.

“Wheee—oh no!” called Cheetah.

Chameleon floated past, upside down. “I changed color from surprise!”

Bear and Papa needed to help their friends.

Chapter 1: The Floaty Rules

Bear reached for Papa’s sleeve.

Papa was drifting sideways, very slowly, like a pancake in a breeze.

“Papa,” Bear said, “we are in space.”

Papa looked around. He saw Turtle bump gently into a wall cushion.

“Bonk,” said Turtle.

He saw George Dino spinning in a slow circle.

“I can see the ceiling,” said George Dino. “Now I can see the floor. Now I can see the ceiling again.”

Cheetah zipped past, waving all four paws. “I am fast! Too fast! I do not know where fast is going!”

Chameleon floated upside down beside a round window. “I feel purple,” he said. Then he turned purple.

Papa took a calm breath. He bent his knees in the air, which looked very funny because there was no floor to bend on.

“First space rule,” Papa said. “Tiny pushes.”

Bear nodded. “No big zooms.”

“Second space rule,” said Papa. “Grab soft handles when you can.”

Bear pointed. Along the walls were blue loops, yellow rails, and soft silver handles.

“Third space rule,” Papa said. “To stop spinning, make your body small, then reach for something steady.”

Bear tucked his arms and legs in like a little astronaut ball. He turned slowly, reached out, and caught a blue loop.

Click.

He stopped.

George Dino gasped. “Bear did science with his body!”

“I did?” said Bear.

“You did,” Papa said warmly. “You changed how you were moving.”

One by one, Bear and Papa helped their friends.

Turtle grabbed a yellow rail. “I am parked.”

George Dino tucked in, reached out, and caught Papa’s paw. “I am not a space tornado anymore.”

Cheetah took three tiny pushes instead of one giant push. “Tiny fast is still fast,” she said proudly.

Chameleon held a silver handle and changed from purple to happy green.

Then Bear spotted something floating near the wall.

“A flashlight!”

It spun gently, blinking on and off.

Bear caught it with both paws. “I can lead us.”

Papa smiled. “Lead the way, Captain Bear.”

Bear shined the flashlight down a round hallway. Everyone followed carefully, handle to handle, rail to rail, tiny push to tiny push.

At the end was a big door that said:

CONTROL ROOM

The door slid open with a soft whoosh.

Inside floated Captain Pippa, a cheetah in a shiny blue space vest. Her tail was looped safely around a chair strap.

“Oh good,” said Captain Pippa. “Helpers.”

Bear held up the flashlight. “We can help.”

Captain Pippa pointed to the control board.

“The station’s Star-Berry Machine is stuck,” she said. “And without star-berries, the bedtime lights won’t glow soft and cozy.”

Everyone went quiet.

Papa looked at Bear.

Bear looked at the blinking buttons.

“We should fix it,” Bear said.

Captain Pippa nodded. “Then we need a plan.”

Chapter 1: The Floaty Rules

Bear reached for Papa’s sleeve.

Papa was drifting sideways, very slowly, like a pancake in a breeze.

“Papa,” Bear said, “we are in space.”

Papa looked around. Turtle bumped gently into a wall cushion.

George Dino was spinning in a slow circle.

“I can see the ceiling,” said George Dino. “Now I can see the floor. Now I can see the ceiling again.”

Cheetah zipped past, waving all four paws. “I am fast! Too fast! I do not know where fast is going!”

Chameleon floated upside down beside a round window. “I feel purple,” he said. Then he turned purple.

Papa held up one paw.

“Space station floaty rule number one,” he said. “Tiny pushes. Big pushes make big zooms.”

Bear nodded. “Tiny pushes. Tiny zooms.”

“Rule number two,” Papa said. “If you are near a handle, grab it gently.”

Bear pointed. Along the walls were blue loops, yellow rails, and soft silver handles.

“Rule number three,” Papa said. “If you are spinning and cannot reach anything, spread out wide like a starfish.”

George Dino stretched out his arms, legs, and tail.

His spin became slower.

“Oooooh,” said George Dino. “I can think again.”

Papa smiled. “That is because spreading out makes spinning slow down. Pulling in small makes spinning speed up, like a skater.”

Bear tried it carefully. He spread wide and turned slowly. Then he tucked in a little and turned faster.

“Whoa,” said Bear. “My body is a spin machine.”

“A very polite spin machine,” Papa said.

“What if I’m facing the wrong way?” asked Cheetah.

Papa wiggled his arms in a slow circle. His body turned the other way a tiny bit.

“In space, moving your arms one way can turn the rest of you the other way,” Papa said. “So: spread out, slow down, wiggle or swing gently to aim, then grab a handle.”

Everyone practiced.

Turtle spread out. “I am a calm space pancake.”

George Dino swung his little arms and turned toward a yellow rail. “I have aimed my dinosaur.”

Cheetah used tiny pushes. “Fast can be careful.”

Chameleon grabbed a silver handle and changed from purple to happy green.

Then Bear spotted something floating near the wall.

“A flashlight!”

It spun gently, blinking on and off.

Bear spread wide, slowed his turn, reached at just the right time, and caught it with both paws.

“I can lead us,” Bear said.

Papa smiled. “Lead the way, Captain Bear.”

Bear shined the flashlight down a round hallway. Everyone followed carefully, handle to handle, rail to rail, tiny push to tiny push.

At the end was a big door that said:

CONTROL ROOM

The door slid open with a soft whoosh.

Inside floated Captain Pippa, a cheetah in a shiny blue space vest. Her tail was looped safely around a chair strap.

“Oh good,” said Captain Pippa. “Helpers.”

Bear held up the flashlight. “We can help.”

Captain Pippa pointed to the control board.

“The station’s Star-Berry Machine is stuck,” she said. “Without star-berries, the bedtime lights won’t glow soft and cozy.”

Everyone looked at the blinking buttons.

Papa looked at Bear.

Bear looked at Papa.

“We should fix it,” Bear said.

Captain Pippa nodded. “Then we need a plan.”

Chapter 2: The Map Before the Mission

Bear looked at the blinking control board.

Then he looked at Turtle.

Then George Dino.

Then Cheetah.

Then Chameleon, who was currently the same color as the blue wall, except for his blinking eyes.

“Wait,” Bear said.

Everyone stopped floating.

“Good wait,” said Papa. “That sounded like a safety wait.”

Bear pointed his flashlight down the hallway. “We should not split up if we do not know the station.”

Captain Pippa nodded. “Very smart. Space stations have lots of doors.”

“And floaty corners,” said Turtle.

“And spinny places,” said George Dino.

“And places where a fast cheetah could become a lost cheetah,” said Cheetah.

Captain Pippa tapped a big green button. A glowing map appeared in the air. It was shaped like three rings connected by tubes.

“This is the station,” she said. “We are here, in the Control Room.”

A little yellow dot blinked.

“The Star-Berry Machine is down here,” Captain Pippa said.

A red dot blinked near the bottom ring.

Papa floated closer. “What are these blue squares?”

“Handle stations,” said Captain Pippa. “Safe places to stop, grab on, and count everyone.”

Bear’s ears perked up. “Count everyone?”

Papa smiled. “That is a good team rule.”

Bear held up his paw. “Before we move, we count.”

He pointed around the room. “Bear. Papa. Turtle. George Dino. Cheetah. Chameleon. Captain Pippa.”

“Seven helpers,” said Turtle.

“Seven not-lost helpers,” said George Dino.

Papa pointed to the map. “Let’s make a simple route. Control Room to Blue Tube. Blue Tube to Snack Window. Snack Window to Star-Berry Machine.”

“Why Snack Window?” asked Cheetah.

“Because it is halfway,” said Papa. “And it has lots of handles.”

“And snacks?” asked Turtle hopefully.

Captain Pippa smiled. “Emergency raisins.”

Turtle whispered, “Excellent station.”

Bear traced the route with his flashlight beam. “We stay together. We use tiny pushes. We stop at every blue square. We count to seven.”

Chameleon turned bright green. “What if someone drifts backward?”

“Then we do not zoom after them,” Papa said. “We say their name calmly. They spread out to slow spinning, aim with gentle arm moves, and grab the nearest handle.”

George Dino practiced spreading wide. “I am a planning star.”

Captain Pippa gave Bear a small wrist light with a button.

“This makes a soft beep,” she said. “Not for emergencies only. It is for teamwork.”

Bear pressed it once.

Beep.

Everyone looked at Bear.

“Good,” said Papa. “One beep means stop and count.”

Bear stood a little taller, even though standing did not work very well in space.

“Team,” Bear said, “to the Snack Window.”

They floated into the Blue Tube.

Tiny push.

Handle.

Tiny push.

Handle.

At the Snack Window, Bear pressed the beep.

Beep.

“Count,” said Bear.

“Bear,” said Bear.

“Papa,” said Papa.

“Turtle,” said Turtle.

“George Dino,” said George Dino.

“Cheetah,” said Cheetah.

“Chameleon,” said Chameleon.

“Captain Pippa,” said Captain Pippa.

Seven.

Everyone was together.

Then, from below, the Star-Berry Machine made a strange sound.

Ker-plunk.

Whirrrr.

Pfffft.

A tiny purple star-berry floated up past the window, wearing a little crown of bubbles.

Captain Pippa gasped. “Oh dear. The machine is not just stuck.”

Bear held the map closer.

Papa’s eyes became thoughtful and kind.

“It is making star-berries float the wrong way,” Papa said.

Bear clicked on his flashlight.

“Then we know where to go,” he said. “Together.”

Chapter 3: How Star-Berries Are Supposed to Float

Bear held up one paw.

“Before we fix it,” he said, “we should know how it works.”

Papa’s eyes twinkled. “That is exactly what a good scientist says.”

Captain Pippa nodded so fast her space vest wiggled. “Right. Normal first. Broken second.”

Everyone held onto the handles near the Snack Window while Captain Pippa opened a floating picture-map of the Star-Berry Machine.

“This,” she said, pointing to a round tank, “is the Berry Bubble Bowl.”

“Ooo,” said Turtle.

“Star-berries grow in there?” asked George Dino.

“Not grow,” said Captain Pippa. “They are mixed there. A tiny scoop of berry dust. A tiny sparkle of sugar. A tiny puff of air. Then the machine wraps each one in a soft bubble so it can float through the station.”

Chameleon turned pink. “Bubble snacks?”

“Bubble bedtime snacks,” said Captain Pippa. “They float to the Cozy Lights. The Cozy Lights glow purple, blue, and gold. Then everyone knows it is rest time.”

Papa tapped the picture-map. “So the machine has three jobs. Mix, bubble, and send.”

Bear counted on his fingers. “Mix. Bubble. Send.”

Captain Pippa pointed to three pipes.

“The red pipe carries berry dust. The silver pipe carries air. The blue pipe carries sleepy sparkle.”

“Sleepy sparkle,” whispered Turtle. “That sounds delicious.”

“It is not for eating plain,” Captain Pippa said gently. “It makes your tongue feel like socks.”

Turtle closed his mouth.

Suddenly the station went:

Ker-plunk.

Whirrrr.

PFFFFT!

Three star-berries floated up from the lower tube.

One was too big.

One was too tiny.

One was perfectly normal except it was wearing a foam mustache.

Cheetah giggled. “That one looks like Papa.”

Papa touched his own face. “My mustache is made of dignity.”

Bear shined his flashlight toward the lower tube. “The machine is mixing wrong.”

“Maybe,” said Papa. “But let’s notice before we decide.”

They moved as a team.

Tiny push.

Handle.

Tiny push.

Handle.

At every blue square, Bear pressed his wrist light.

Beep.

“Count,” he said.

“Bear.”

“Papa.”

“Turtle.”

“George Dino.”

“Cheetah.”

“Chameleon.”

“Captain Pippa.”

Seven.

At last they reached the Star-Berry Room.

It was round and shiny. In the middle sat the Star-Berry Machine. It had a clear bowl, three pipes, four dials, and one very worried-looking blinking light.

The machine went:

Glub.

Foop.

Pfffft!

A bubble popped out and bonked George Dino gently on the nose.

“I have been berried,” he said.

Bear floated close, but not too close.

“Safety first,” said Papa.

Bear nodded. “No paws inside moving machines.”

Captain Pippa clipped everyone to a soft safety line attached to the wall. “Now nobody drifts into the machine.”

Bear looked through the clear bowl.

Inside, berry dust swirled in clumps. Air bubbles came too fast. Blue sleepy sparkle dripped in slow, sleepy blobs.

“That does not look like mix, bubble, send,” Bear said.

Papa smiled. “Good noticing.”

Cheetah squinted at the dials. “This red dial says berry dust. This silver dial says air. This blue dial says sparkle.”

Turtle pointed. “The silver dial is jiggling.”

The silver air dial shook back and forth.

Chameleon turned yellow with concentration. “Maybe too much air makes too many bubbles.”

Captain Pippa checked her normal-work picture. “The air dial should be at two dots.”

Bear looked at the dial. “It is at five dots!”

George Dino gasped. “Five is more than two!”

“By three,” said Papa.

“That is many bubbles,” said Turtle.

The machine puffed again.

PFFFFT!

This time a crowd of bubbles burst out and started pushing everyone gently backward.

“Spread out!” called Bear.

Everyone spread their arms and legs wide. Their spinning slowed. Cheetah, who had begun to turn sideways, swung her arms carefully one way, and her body turned the other way just enough to face a handle.

“Got it!” she said, grabbing on.

Papa looked proud. “Beautiful space thinking.”

Bear held the safety line and floated closer to the control dials. “We need to turn air from five dots to two dots.”

“But the dial is shaking,” said Captain Pippa. “It might be stuck.”

Papa opened the tool pouch. “Then we do not yank. We steady.”

Bear picked a soft clamp tool. It looked like a little padded crab claw.

“I can hold the dial still,” Bear said.

“And I can turn it slowly,” said Captain Pippa.

Papa nodded. “Turtle, watch the bowl. George Dino, count the dots. Cheetah, watch the safety line. Chameleon, tell us if the sleepy sparkle changes.”

Everyone got ready.

Bear clipped the soft clamp around the silver dial.

“Steady,” he said.

Captain Pippa turned it.

Click.

“Four dots!” said George Dino.

The machine rumbled.

Click.

“Three dots!”

The bubbles slowed.

Click.

“Two dots!”

The Star-Berry Machine became quiet.

Then it made a soft, happy sound.

Hum-hum-hummm.

Inside the bowl, berry dust swirled smoothly. Air bubbles popped in gentle little pearls. Sleepy sparkle made tiny blue curls.

“Mix,” whispered Bear.

“Bubble,” whispered Papa.

“Send,” whispered Captain Pippa.

One perfect star-berry floated out.

Then another.

Then another.

They drifted into the Cozy Light tube, and the space station lamps began to glow purple, blue, and gold.

Turtle sighed. “That is a very bedtime color.”

Captain Pippa smiled at Bear and Papa. “You fixed the Star-Berry Machine.”

Bear smiled too. “We all fixed it.”

Then the machine gave one last little hiccup.

Plip.

A star-berry floated out bigger than all the others.

It was not dangerous.

But it was so big that it slowly blocked the Cozy Light tube like a soft purple pillow.

Captain Pippa blinked. “Oh.”

Papa chuckled gently. “One small final problem.”

Bear clicked on his flashlight. “Team count first.”

Beep.

Seven helpers held their handles and looked at the big squishy star-berry.

Chapter 4: The Softest Space Problem

Bear pointed to the giant star-berry blocking the Cozy Light tube.

“Turtle,” he said, “you are good at steady.”

Turtle blinked slowly. “I am extremely steady.”

“Can you cushion it so it does not wobble around while I check the machine?”

Turtle gave a proud little nod. Captain Pippa clipped Turtle to a soft safety line. Then Turtle floated to the tube, spread his arms wide, and gently hugged the giant star-berry.

“It is like holding a sleepy balloon,” Turtle said.

The berry squished softly.

“Careful,” said Papa. “Gentle holding. No squeezing.”

“I am a pillow with manners,” said Turtle.

Bear floated back to the Star-Berry Machine. Papa stayed beside him.

“What made it big?” Bear asked.

Papa smiled. “Let’s do scientist steps.”

Bear counted on his fingers. “Notice. Ask how. Test safely. Then fix.”

They looked at the machine.

The air dial was still at two dots.

“The dial is right,” said Bear.

The berry dust pipe puffed normally.

“The red pipe is right,” said Captain Pippa.

Chameleon leaned close to the blue pipe. He turned blue from nose to tail. “Sleepy sparkle is still blobbing.”

Bear frowned gently. “The sparkle is coming out in globs, not curls.”

Papa nodded. “When the sleepy sparkle comes in blobs, it may wrap one berry too much.”

“So it made one berry wear too many pajamas?” asked George Dino.

“Exactly,” said Papa. “Very scientific pajamas.”

Bear shined his flashlight behind the blue pipe. There, half-hidden, was a tiny valve with a sticky label on it.

The label said:

SLOW DRIPPER

But the valve was stuck halfway.

Bear did not touch it yet.

“No paws in moving parts,” he said.

Papa handed him a small safe tool with a soft rubber tip.

Bear gently tapped the side of the valve.

Tap.

Nothing.

Tap tap.

The valve wiggled.

Chameleon watched the blue pipe. “The blobs are getting smaller.”

Bear tapped once more.

Tap.

The valve clicked into place.

Inside the bowl, the sleepy sparkle changed from blobs to tiny blue curls.

Captain Pippa clapped softly. “That is normal!”

Then Turtle called, “Polite pillow needs a plan!”

The giant star-berry was still blocking the tube.

Bear looked at the berry. Then the tube. Then the little air puff nozzle nearby.

“We should not push it hard,” Bear said. “It might bounce.”

Papa’s eyes twinkled. “So?”

“Tiny puffs,” said Bear. “Like tiny pushes.”

Captain Pippa set the nozzle to one dot.

Puff.

The giant berry moved a little.

Turtle guided it with gentle paws.

Puff.

It slid free.

Puff.

It floated into a wide basket beside the Cozy Light tube and settled there with a soft plump sound.

Floomp.

The Cozy Lights brightened all around the station.

Purple.

Blue.

Gold.

Everyone’s faces glowed warm and sleepy.

Bear pressed his wrist light.

Beep.

They counted.

“Bear.”

“Papa.”

“Turtle.”

“George Dino.”

“Cheetah.”

“Chameleon.”

“Captain Pippa.”

Seven helpers. All safe.

Captain Pippa smiled. “The station is cozy again.”

George Dino yawned. “My brain feels like bedtime soup.”

Papa chuckled. “Then the machine is working perfectly.”

Chapter 5: The Sleeping Star Trick

The Cozy Lights glowed soft purple, blue, and gold.

The Star-Berry Machine hummed like it was singing with its mouth closed.

Hum-hum-hummm.

Papa looked at the sleepy team.

Turtle was blinking slowly. George Dino’s tail floated like a noodle. Cheetah was trying very hard not to zoom. Chameleon had turned the exact color of a yawn.

“One last floating trick,” Papa said.

Bear held a handle and listened.

“This is called the Sleeping Star,” said Papa. “First, spread out wide.”

Everyone stretched gently.

“Spreading out slows your spin,” Bear said.

Papa nodded. “Then take one slow breath.”

Everyone breathed in.

And out.

“Now look where you want to go,” Papa said. “Move your arms gently to turn. Tiny push. Then grab or clip in.”

Bear tried it first.

He spread out wide, breathed slowly, turned with a small arm move, gave one tiny push, and caught the sleep-room handle.

Click.

“Sleeping Star complete,” Bear whispered.

One by one, the friends followed.

Turtle floated in like a very careful moon.

George Dino bumped a pillow and said, “I meant to do that quietly.”

Cheetah used the tiniest push of all. “I am fast asleep already,” she murmured.

Chameleon clipped in beside a glowing wall and turned soft blanket-blue.

Captain Pippa smiled. “Seven helpers?”

Bear pressed his wrist light one last time.

Beep.

They counted softly.

“Bear.”

“Papa.”

“Turtle.”

“George Dino.”

“Cheetah.”

“Chameleon.”

“Captain Pippa.”

Seven safe friends.

Papa tucked a soft strap around Bear’s blanket so he would not drift away.

Bear looked through the round window. Stars shone quietly outside.

“We helped,” Bear said.

“We did,” Papa said. “We learned the station, stayed together, and used careful science.”

Bear’s eyes grew heavy.

The Cozy Lights glowed warm and low.

The station hummed.

And all the helpers drifted into soft, safe, starry rest.
